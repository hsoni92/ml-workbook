Stage 5 is deployment. This is where
model engineering starts to become very
central. Deployment means taking the
trained model and exposing it so other
systems can call it. That might be the
online API for real-time predictions,
or a batch job that writes output once
a day. Integrating those predictions
into the product experience, for
example, a ranking algorithm. In a feed
or a fraud score used by other service?
Once we're here, we can't just think
about accuracy, we must also care about
latency, how quickly we respond, up
time and reliability, how often the
service is available, cost and security,
how expensive and how safe it is to run
the model at scale. This is where a lot
of work in this course will focus. Once
a model is deployed.
We move to monitoring. Monitoring
covers multiple layers. System health,
we are in within latency target. Are
error rates spiking? Is the service up?
Data quality and drift? Are the input
distributions changing? Are we seeing
new categories, missing values, or
unexpected pattern? Model performance,
where we have ground truth or delayed
labels, are our predictions still
accurate over time? The goal is to
catch issues earlier, ideally before
users notice degraded quality. Without
good monitoring, production ML systems
tend to silently degrade. The last
stage in the life cycle is retraining
and depreciation. Over time, the world
changes, user behaviour shifts, the
model may start performing worse.
That's model drift.
To keep performance good. We need to
retrain with fresh data, possibly with
updated features or model architectures.
In some cases, we may decide to
deprecate a model. Maybe the use case
is no longer important.
Or the model's complexity and cost are
no longer justified? By its benefits.
Retraining and depreciation are part of
keeping our ML system healthy and
sustainable over the long term, so the
life cycle is not a line, it's more
like a loop. If we look at the life
cycle as a whole, it's not a straight
line, it's a loop. After we deploy a
model, we monitor it in production,
then retrain
or update based on what we see. And
deploy again. Feedback come from user
behaviour, product metrics, and new or
changed data. Successful ML systems are
usually the result of many small
iterations around this loop, rather
than a single big launch. This loop,
deployment, monitoring, retraining. Is
where ML Model Engineering spends most
of its time.
