Next, let's talk about dashboards. A
good rule of thumb is.
One primary dashboard per important
model.
That dashboard should show.
Top level system health, traffic,
latency, error rates, so you know the
API or the batch job is not on fire.
Data health, drift scores and schema,
missing value summaries for your top
features. Prediction health. Recent
model metrics compared to a baseline.
Plus segment breakdown, to catch
fairness issues. Design, design it so
that. Someone can scan it in 30 seconds
and answer.
Is this model basically fine? Or, is
something clearly wrong that deserves a
deeper look? Always highlight
exceptions.
Out of range values, red or amber
indicators. Rather than forcing people
to read Wall of Numbers.
Dashboards Are for pull monitoring.
People look when they are curious.
Alerts are push monitoring. The system
taps you on the shoulder.
To turn metrics into alerts, define
conditions tied up to your objectives.
P. 95 latency above X
for more than Y minutes, alert. Drift
score above a threshold for several
hours in a row. Alert. Model
performance on fresh label, failing or
falling more than delta below the
baseline. To avoid alert fatigue,
trigger on sustained issues, not single
noisy spikes. Require a metric to come
back inside the band before an alert is
fully resolved.
Send the message emails for
investigation.
Alerts
And reserve pagers for truly urgent or
take action now situations. And,
crucially, each alert should include a
link to a runbook, a short document
describing. What to check next?
Monitoring only works if someone owns
the response. In most, our
responsibility is split roughly like
the following data engineering handles
schema change, missing data, pipeline
failure. Model or ML engineers handle
drift, performance degradation, and
retraining decisions. Platform or infra
teams handle latency, CPU, scaling, and
network issues. For each alert type you
want a primary owner, the person or
team expected to respond first. A
secondary or escalation path, and a
clear destination, a slack or team's
message, on call rotation or ticket
queue. When an alert fires, it should
be obvious who is on the hook for
triage. A run book is a short playbook
for each alert type. It typically
includes. One line explanation of what
that alert means in business terms. A
list of common causes schema change,
traffic spikes, roll out issues.
Any such thing A sequence of checks.
Which dashboard to open, which log to
inspect, which queries to run. And a
menu of Next action. Roll back to a
previous model, disable a feature. Open
a ticket for retraining or escalate to
other team. The benefit is huge. Triage
as faster.
People panic less. And new team members
don't have to reinvent the process
every time something goes wrong.
