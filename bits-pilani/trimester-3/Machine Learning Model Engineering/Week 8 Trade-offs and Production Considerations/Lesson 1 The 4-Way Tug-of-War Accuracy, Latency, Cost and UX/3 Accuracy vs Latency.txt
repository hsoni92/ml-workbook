Let's talk about each force, starting
with accuracy. Accuracy is obviously
important. Better models detect fraud
more reliably, rank search results more
effectively, provide safer or fairer
decisions. But in many architectures,
getting higher accuracy means building
deeper networks, increasing the numbers
of parameters, and increasing the cost
per request. At some point, each extra
percentage point of accuracy comes with
large costs in terms of latency and
infrastructure.
That is where we need to start asking,
is this extra accuracy actually worth
what it does to latency and cost?
Next, latency. Latency is the time
between sending a request and getting a
prediction back. Human perception is
very sensitive here.
Under about 100 to 200 millisecond
interaction feel almost instant.
Between 0.5 2nd and a second, people
start to feel that there is a delay.
Beyond 1 2nd, the interface can feel
slow and frustrating. Notice something
similar or something important. User
never see a metric like area under the
curve, or AUC. They simply feel whether
the product is responsive.
That is why latency is a such a
powerful and sometimes invisible force
in this tug of war.
Now let us put accuracy and latency
together. Imagine you have 2 candidates.
Model A has 11 or 2% points higher
accuracy.
But it is 3 times slower. Model B is
slightly less accurate, but much faster.
In an offline leaderboard, Model A
looks like a winner, but in production,
Model B might be the right choice if.
Model A would break your P95 latency
target. Model B keeps the experience
snappy. And Model B allows you to
handle more traffic on the same amount
of hardware. This is why. Model
engineering often choose
a model that is good enough, accurate,
but much better on latency and cost.
The third force is cost. Cost is driven
by how many machines you run, whether
you use expensive GPUs or cheaper CPUs,
storage or network usage, and
engineering work to maintain the system.
Pushing latency fewer often means more
replicas of the service or more
powerful and expensive instances. So a
natural question for every change is.
Is the extra accuracy, or the lower
latency, actually worth the increase in
monthly cloud bills?
Sometimes the answer is yes, sometimes
the answer is not for this product or
this segment of users.
The fourth force is user experience,
often shortened as UX. UX is a
combination of how fast the system
feels, how often it is right, how often
it fails or time out, and whether user
feels they can trust it.
This is what ultimately matter to the
product and the business. The system
that is perfectly accurate but
painfully slow as bad UX. A system that
is fast but often wrong also has a bad
UX.
Sometimes the best UX comes from
accepting slightly lower accuracy,
investing more in latency or
reliability, or even spending a bit
more money on critical user flows. UX
is where all the other forces become
visible.
