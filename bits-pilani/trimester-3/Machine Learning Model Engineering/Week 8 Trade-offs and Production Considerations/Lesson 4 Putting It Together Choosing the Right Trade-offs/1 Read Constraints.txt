Let us start with a quick recap.
Every production machine learning
system lives in a 4 dimensional space.
Accuracy, how often the model is right,
latency, how fast the model responds.
Cost. Both infrastructure and
engineering effort. And user experience.
Often shorthand for UX.
Which is how all of this feels to the
users.
In real life, you cannot simply
maximise all four at once. Instead, you
choose a balance between fits the model.
Or the product needs and the business
constraints.
This video is about making that choice
more systematic. The first step is not
to choose a scaling pattern or a model
format. The first step is to read the
constraints. For every system, ask.
Latency and user experience. How
sensitive. Is the UXR latency. Do we
have strict service level agreements,
such as P95 latency?
Under 2 milliseconds.
Accuracy and risk. What happens when
the model is wrong? Is this about
safety, money, or is it relatively a
trois?
Cost envelope.
What kind of budget do we have?
Are we optimising cost per request? Or
just making sure the bills stays within
a certain range. Traffic pattern. Is
the traffic constant or spiky?
Are we dealing with low volume or very
high queries per second? Often
abbreviated as QPS.
Answer these questions. We will drive
all of other design choices. Let us
zoom into the latency and user
experience dimensions.
If a human is literally waiting on the
response during the log in. At checkout,
while scrolling a feed. Then we are in
online request response territory.
We usually have latency targets such as
P95 under 100 to 200 milliseconds.
If no one is waiting, for example, in
monthly churn prediction or nightly
backfill, then we can treat the
workload as batch or asynchronous.
Individual request latency is less
important, and we can focus on
throughput and cost. Tight UX
constraint push us toward faster model,
more replicas or better scaling, and
sometimes specialised hardware, such as
GPUs combined with micro batching.
Next, think about accuracy and risk.
Some system are high stakes, fraud
detection, on payments, medical
decisions, support, safety or legal
decisions.
In these cases, false negatives and
false positives can be extremely
expensive or dangerous.
Other are media mistakes, such as
pricing, ranking and promotions.
Mistakes hurts revenue and user trust.
But they are not life critical. And
some are relatively low stake, such as
content recommendation or simple
personalization. Here, mistakes are
mostly just annoying.
The higher the stakes, the more you
lean toward. High accuracy, stronger
monitoring, gradual rollout like canary
deployments, and sometimes more
explainability requirement.
Finally,
Add traffic patterns and cost envelope
to the picture.
If traffic is constant and high volume,
you usually want dedicated service
running on a cluster.
With auto schooling. To adjust capacity.
And a lot of focus on keeping P95
latency stable. If traffic is spiky or
low volume, it may be cheaper and
simpler to use serverless inference or
a small cluster that scales down
aggressively.
With a strict cost cap, you might use
spot instances for batch workload. Rely
on batching or micro batching to get
more value out of GPUs.
And when model compression to shrink
footprints. If the budget is more
flexible, you can push harder on
latency and reliability for key user
flows.
