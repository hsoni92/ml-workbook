Let us make this concrete. With few
scenarios. Scenario one. A payment
fraud check. Constraints, very high
accuracy and very high stake. Mistakes
either lose money or block legitimate
users.
Low latency, because the user is
waiting for the payment to go through,
and high availability, the system
should rarely, if ever, be down. A like
design, a compact but strong model,
perhaps a distilled or optimised
version of a larger model. Online
inference with strict service level
agreement, horizontal scaling without
auto scaling based on latency and
queries per second, possibly GPU and
high-end CPU instances, and
aggressively monitoring latency, error
drifts, and business metrics. Here we
are willing to pay more for
infrastructure to protect accuracy,
latency and user experience.
Scenario 2. Monthly churn prediction.
Here, no one is waiting in real time.
You might have a time window to a few
hours to run the job. And the data
volume can be very large.
A likely design, pure batch inference,
over many rows at once. Using spot
instances or preemptible instances to
save cost, potentially using heavier
model because latency per user is less
than total throughput, focusing on rows
per second and total job runtime. The
key user experience quo question here
is not milliseconds, it is does the
churn. Scoring finish in time for the
business to act on the results.
Scenario 3. A mobile camera app running
inference on device constraints, the
model must run on phone or edge device.
Latency and battery usage must be low,
memory and compute are limited. A
likely design A compressed or quantized
model. Exported to a format like
Tensorflow Light or ONNX, that is
supported on device. Often a distilled
student model that mimics a larger
teacher trained in the cloud.
With a strong focus on model size in
megabytes, power usage, and latency per
frame. The cloud still plays a role.
Training the large teacher model,
generating new student model,
collecting aggregated analytics. Here,
accuracy is balanced between device
constraint and end user smoothness.
We can turn everything into compact
decision flow. Step one.
Is a user waiting? If yes, choose an
online, low latency setup. If no,
Consider batch or asynchronous
processing. Step 2. How risky are
mistakes? If the risk is high,
prioritise accuracy, strong monitoring
and safe roll out pattern like canaries.
If the risk is lower, you have more
room to compress the model and choose
simpler architecture.
Step 3, traffic pattern and budget. For
high, steady traffic, use dedicated
servers.
With horizontal scaling and auto
scaling. For spiky and low traffic,
serverless or a small cluster plus spot
instances can be more cost effective.
Step 4, fit the model. And optimisation.
To those choices. Pick the right model
size and compression level. Choose
hardware, CPU, GPU, or edge devices.
Decide whether to use batching or micro
batching. This is not a perfect formula,
but it gives you a structured way to
reason about tradeoff.
