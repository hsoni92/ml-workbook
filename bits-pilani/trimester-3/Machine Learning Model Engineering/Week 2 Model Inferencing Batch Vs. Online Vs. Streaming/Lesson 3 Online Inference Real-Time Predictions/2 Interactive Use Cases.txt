So when is online inference the right
tool? Think about a scenario like
searching and ranking. When a user
types a query, we want to rank and
philtre results in real time.
personalised recommendations. When a
user lands
on a homepage or product page, we want
to serve suggestions tailored to that
movement.
Real time fraud or risk checks. At
checkouts, login or password reset, we
might want to block suspicious activity
before it goes through. Dynamic UX and
personalization, content layout or
pricing that adapt to the current user
and context. The common theme, there is
a live interaction happening. And
something important doesn't move
forward until the prediction arrives,
that's when you're almost certainly in
online inference territory.
Here's the mental shift to keep in mind.
In online inference, the user is
waiting. That means, model latency
directly affects page load time. How
snappy the app feels. Abandonment rates.
Example like user dropping out of
checkout funnel because it feels too
slow. You might have product
requirement like show recommendation in
under 100 or 200 milliseconds, complete
a fraud check, and approve or decline
the payment before payment provider's
timeout.
So for online inference, metrics like
P95 and P99 latency are not just
engineering stats, they become product
requirement. If we miss them, the user
experience breaks. Let's connect this
to our inference matrix dashboard for
online systems. The metrics we care
more about are average or P50 latency
to understand the general.
Typical performance, P95 and P99
latency. To understand the worst
experience that user will feel, error
rate. How often do we time out, return
errors, or fall back to default?
We still care about throughput, how
many requests per second we can handle,
and cost, specifically at peak traffic,
where every extra millisecond of
computation gets multiplied by
thousands of requests. But the headline
for online inference is, can we meet
the latency SLOs, specifically at P95
and P99, even when traffic spikes.
