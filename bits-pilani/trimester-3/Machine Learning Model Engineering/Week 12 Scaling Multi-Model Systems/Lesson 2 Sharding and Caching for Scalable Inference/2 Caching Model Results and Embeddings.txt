Shading simply mean splitting things up.
In a sharded system, each shard is
responsible for a subset of your
traffic or data, maybe a subset of
users, items, or regions. Each shard
has its own resources, its own
instances, storages, and sometimes its
own model version.
Shading helps with scalability because
each shard is smaller and easier to
handle, and it also gives you some
isolation. A spike or failure on one
shard doesn't necessarily take down the
others. Inside each shard, you can
still be replication or do replication
if you need multiple instances for
reliability or extra throughput.
One common sharding strategy is hashba
sharding, often by user ID. For example,
you might compute shard is equal to
hash of user ID mod N. And route the
request to that shard.
With a good hash, users are spread
roughly evenly. Across the sharks.
Which gives you fairly balanced load.
The advantage here is, it's simple and
work well when user behaves similarly.
The disadvantage, it's harder to do per
region or per language customization,
because users from the same region may
end up on different shard. And changing
the number of shards, say, from 3 to 4,
can force you to rebalance, moving lots
of users between the shards. Another
pattern of sharding by a meaningful
attribute like region, language, or
customer segment. For example, you
might route all Europe users to a shard
that hosts a model tuned to Europe data
and regulations. And all Indian users
to a shard tuned to local patterns.
Or you might have a dedicated chart for
enterprise versus small business
customers. The big benefit is that it
becomes easier to specialise model and
infrastructure per shard. You can have
different configurations, SLOs, or even
different model architectures. The
downside is that shards might not be
perfectly balanced. One region or
segment might carry much more traffic,
so you have to watch for hot shards and
plan capacity accordingly. It's
important to distinguish replication
from shading.
With replication You have multiple
copies of same model, all capable of
handling the same kind of traffic. A
load balancer spreads requests across
these replicas to increase throughput
and reliability. With sharding,
different shards handle different
subsets of traffic or data. Shards
might even run different models or
configurations. In real system. You
often combine both. For example, you
might have 3 shards by reason, and
within each shard you run 3 replicas
for reliability, so a total of 9
instances.
