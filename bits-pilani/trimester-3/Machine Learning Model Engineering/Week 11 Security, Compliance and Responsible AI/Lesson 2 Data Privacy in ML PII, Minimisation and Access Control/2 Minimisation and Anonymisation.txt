A core privacy principle is data
minimization. Don't hoard data just
because you can. Collect only what you
need for the specific task and metric
you're targeting. Ask questions like.
Do we actually need raw identifiers, or
would an internal ID suffice?
Can we use aggregated or bucketed
features instead of raw values? Can we
safely drop or archive data after a
certain time of window?
minimising data reduces your attack
surface, simplifies compliance, and
makes accidental misuse less likely.
It's one of the simplest and most
powerful privacy tool you have.
To reduce privacy risk, we use a few
standard tools masking or redaction
heights, parts of sensitive fields, for
instance, storing only the last four
digits of a number.
Aggregation or bucketing group values,
we store age ranges instead of exact
ages, or city level location instead of
precise GPS coordinates.
Pseudonormalization replaces direct
identifiers with internal IDs or hashes,
while the mapping is stored separately
in the restricted system. These
techniques help but full.
Anonymity is hard to guarantee.
Especially when data can be cross
referenced with other sources, so treat
anonymization as a risk reduction tool,
not magic. You will sometime hear,
Don't worry, we'll just anonymize it.
In practise, that's tricky. Many
machine learning tasks genuinely need
detailed behaviour, long histories,
rare events, fine grained context,
reducing everything to course bucket.
And destroy signals.
On top of that, data sets can be joined.
When look what look anonymous within
your system might become identifying
when combined with external data, and
even model artefacts can leak
information if they memorise rare
examples. The takeaway is, don't rely
on anonymizations alone. Combine data
minimization, technical controls like
access controls and encryption, and
organisational policies. Thank you.
