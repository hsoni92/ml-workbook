Here are a few practical fairness
questions you can ask.
Is one group systematically getting
more false negatives, for example. Are
certain applicant or applicants
disproportionately denied loans, even
when they should qualify?
Does model performance drop sharply for
some subpopulation, like smaller
language groups or certain age bands?
Is the model calibrated similarly
across groups? Does a score of 0.8 mean
roughly the same real world probability
for Group A and Group B?
And are there thresholds or decision
rules that in practise benefit one
group more than
another? Just asking these questions
and looking at the numbers is a big
step forward, much easier to discuss
with non-machine learning stakeholders.
Use simple bar charts to compare
accuracy, precision, or recall across
groups. Use grouped bars or line charts
to show false positives and false
negative rates. Calibration plots can
reveal that, for example, a score of
0.8 means different actual
probabilities for different groups. In
tables, consider showing both the raw
value and the difference. For example,
Group B accuracy is 7% points lower
than Group A.
Highlight cells where gaps exceed a
chosen threshold, like 5% percentage
point, so they stand out.
This makes fairness and observable
behaviour, not a fuzzy feeling.
