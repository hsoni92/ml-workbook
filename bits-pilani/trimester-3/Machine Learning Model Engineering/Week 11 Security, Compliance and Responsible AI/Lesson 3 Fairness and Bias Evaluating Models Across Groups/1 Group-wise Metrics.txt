Welcome to Module 11, Topic 3. Fairness
and bias, evaluating Models Across
Group. Even when a model looks good, on
average, it can behave very differently
for different groups of people.
Some may systematically get worse
predictions. Or more harmful errors. In
this video. We're not trying to solve
ethics and law in 8 minutes. Instead,
we'll focus on a very practical skill,
how to evaluate model
behaviour across groups and make
disparities visible and measurable.
This will set you up for the lab where
you will compute some of these metrics
yourself. Fairness means different
things in different context. In this
course, we use a working definition. We
are mainly asking questions like, does
the model behave consistently across
groups?
Are some groups getting systematically
worse errors? To answer that, we break
the data into segments, for example,
Group A versus Group B, and compare
metrics and error rates for each
segment. The numbers helps us see
patterns, deciding what to do about
those patterns still needs domain
expertise, policy, and context. The
model engineer's job is to surface the
behaviour clearly.
The core technique is group comparison.
First, you choose a group attribute. It
might be region, age band, product year,
or where allowed and appropriate, a
sensitive attribute like gender or
ethnicity. Then you slice your
evaluation data into subsets.
One for each group, for each subset,
you compute the same metrics you
already know. Accuracy, precision,
recall, AUC, and so on. Finally, you
compare the matrix across groups and
ask.
Are differences small and expected
given the context, or are they large
enough to be worrying, especially for
high impact decisions?
This simple pattern is at the heart of
many fairness checks. Here is a simple
example. Suppose your classifier has
92% accuracy for Group A and 83%
accuracy for Group B. If you only
looked at the overall accuracy, you
might see something like 90% and feel
great, but once you split by group, you
see that Group B consistently gets
worse prediction. That might matter a
lot, especially if Group B represents a
vulnerable population, or a key
customer segment. The next step is to
break this down into error types, false
positives and false negatives, to
understand how the model is failing for
Group B. Accuracy alone hides important
details. False positives and false
negatives often matter more. A false
positive is when the model predicts
positive but the truth is negative, for
example, flagging a legitimate
transaction as fraud. A false negative
is when the model predicts negative,
but the truth is positive. For example,
missing an actual fraudulent
transaction. Different domain care
about different errors. In fraud, you
might tolerate more false positives. In
healthcare or hiring, false negatives
might be more painful.
Fairness quotient often looks like, are
false positive or false negative rates
significantly higher for one group or
another?
