A few important caveats. There is not a
single universal fairness metric.
You can talk about equal accuracy,
equal false positive, equal false
negative rates, equal calibrations, and
more, and these definitions can
conflict with each other.
Trying to improve one fairness measure
may cost you some overall accuracy or
add complexity that affect latency or
cost. And metrics alone cannot tell you
what's acceptable. Deciding whether a
3% gap or a 10% gap is OK depends on
domain, stakes, policy, and regulation.
So treat fairness numbers as a single
or discussion and investigation, not a
magic yes no verdict. So what's your
role as model engineer? First. Make
group wise matrics easy to compute,
through script, notebook, or dashboards.
If it's easy, people are more likely to
do it regularly. Second, Flag large or
surprising gaps early in the life cycle,
not right before launch. Third,
document which groups you evaluated and
gaps you saw, and what decisions were
made in response. This helps features.
Teams understand the context and shows
the fairness was considered. And
finally, remember, you're not alone.
Work with domain experts, legal, policy
and product teams. Your job is to
provide clear evidence and options, not
to decide fairness in isolation. To
summarise, fairness checks. Begin with

stop at overall accuracy. Look at false
