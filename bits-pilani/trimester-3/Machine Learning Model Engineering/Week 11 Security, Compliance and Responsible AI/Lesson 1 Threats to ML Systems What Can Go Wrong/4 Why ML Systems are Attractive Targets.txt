So why are machine learning systems
particularly attractive targets? First,
they often sit close to high stake
decisions money, access, safety,
reputation. That means any successful
attack can have outsized impact. Second,
model behaviour is complex and
nonintuitive. Even honest team
struggles to predict every age edge
cases. Attackers can take advantage of
that by exploring the system with
carefully chosen inputs. Third, the
attack surface is broad. Data pipeline,
feature stores, batch jobs, online APIs,
dashboards, and logs. A weakness in one
of these can affect the whole system.
And if your deployment and testing
processes are immature, fixes can be
slow. Leaving you exposed longer. The
takeaway, machine learning security is
not separate from MLOs. It's another
reason to have good automation,
monitoring and discipline. What can you
do as a model engineer?
You don't have to solve security alone,
but you can design with defence in mind.
A few first line practises, implement
input validation and rate limiting, so
obviously bad and excessive traffic is
rejected early.
Apply least privilege to data, feature
stores and model, not everyone needs
production data access or model
downloads rights.
Use the monitoring tool we built in
early modules to watch for anomalies in
inputs, outputs, and traffic patterns.
Choose secure defaults, disable debug
endpoints by default, keep error
messages minimal, and rely on version.
Auditable deployments through your CICD
pipeline. These don't remove all risk,
but they dramatically raise the bar for
potential attacks.
