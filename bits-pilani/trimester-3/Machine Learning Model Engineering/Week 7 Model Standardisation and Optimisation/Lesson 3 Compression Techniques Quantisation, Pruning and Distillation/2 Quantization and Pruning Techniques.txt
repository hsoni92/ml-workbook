First technique is quantization.
Most deep learning model is trained
using 32 bit floating point numbers, or
FP 32.
With quantization, we reduce the number
of bits of use to store and compute,
for example, from F3 FP 32 to FP 16,
and even to 8 bit integer like in 8.
When we do that, the model file gets
smaller. And move less data in and out
of memory. And many CPUs and GPUs can
run low precision math much faster. The
catch is, we're approximating numbers.
So we might see a small drop in
accuracy.
A lot of this art is in getting most of
the speed and size benefit while
keeping accuracy within acceptable
limit.
In practise, quantization comes in two
main flavours, Post-training
quantization, also called as PTQ. You
train a model in F. P 32 model.
Normal FP 32 model. And then you
quantize it after training.
Often using a small calibration data
set.
It's easy to try, and you don't need to
retrain the model. The downside is that
you might lose more accuracy,
specifically at very low precision like
in 8.
Quantization aware training. Or, also
called as QAT.
You simulate quantization trade during
training. The model learns to do robust
to round and low precision effects. If,
it requires more training effort, but
usually gives better accuracy at the
same bit width. A common pattern is,
try PTQ first and if you need more
aggressive compression with better
accuracy, consider QAT.
