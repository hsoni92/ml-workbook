With this, finally, we'll talk about
positioning. Positioning can be
understood by looking at how a business
defines itself in the minds of
consumers, either around the product it
offers or the market value it delivers.
In a product-oriented definition, the
focus is on what the company makes or
sells. In a market-oriented definition,
the focus shifts on the value, the
experience, the benefit the consumer
receives. Look at some of the real
world examples of how brands use these
concepts of positioning, and let's
understand the differences.
For example, Starbucks follows a
product orientation based approach. The
definition over here is selling coffee
and snacks, while market-oriented
definition would focus on delivering
the Starbucks experiences. For Walmart,
the product focuses on running discount
stores, and the market-oriented
definition emphasises on low prices and
better living.
Let's take some more examples. For Zoom,
the shift is from hosting online
meetings to enable frictionless and
secure video communication. For HOL,
the focus moves from making soaps and
detergents, helping people feel good,
look good, and get more out of their
life. Let's take some more examples.
With Strain, the product oriented
definition is making air conditioners,
while the market-oriented definition
would simply be offering comfort. In
Big Bazaar's case, the product focus is
running hypermarkets to sell household
merchandise, while the market-oriented
definition emphasises offering the best
practises or khan. Final example for
Airbnb, the product-oriented definition
is renting places online, while the
market-oriented definition is helping
people belong anywhere. And at Life
Insurance Corporation of India LIC, the
product definition is about selling
insurance, while the market-oriented
definition. Assures of financial
support with life after life. These
examples show how positioning moves
from a product focus to a market and a
consumer value focus.
