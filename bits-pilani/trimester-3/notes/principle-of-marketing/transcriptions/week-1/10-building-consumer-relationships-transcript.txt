Welcome to this next lesson. In this
video, we'll explore how to build
consumer relationships with the right
consumers, focusing on the balance
between projected loyalty and potential
profitability. Understanding these two
concepts is crucial because it helps
businesses prioritise their efforts and
resources to foster long-term growth.
Let's begin by looking into the four
relational groups strangers,
butterflies, true friends, and
barnacles. These groups are categorised
based on their projected loyalty and
projected profitability. The first
group we'll discuss is strangers. These
are consumers who have low potential
profitability and low potential loyalty.
In other words, there is little chance
they will stick around for the long
term. They aren't contributing too much
to the bottom line at the moment. As
marketeers, it's crucial not to invest
too much into this group since their
loyalty and profitability are low. It's
best to try to make money on every
transaction from them instead of
focusing on long-term relationship with
them. Think of strangers as one time
customers, even if they come back,
great, but there's no reason to spend
significant resources trying to convert
them to repeat customers. Next we have
butterflies. These consumers have high
potential profitability but low
potential loyalty. These are the
customers who are attracted to a brand
or to your product or service for a
short time. But the interest may not
last. A great example of butterflies is
a stock trader who uses different
brokerage apps to trade at different
times, enjoying the experience for a
short while, but not necessarily
sticking to one brand. Efforts to
convert butterflies into loyal
consumers are unsuccessful in most of
the cases because they tend to shop
around and then they don't stay
committed long term. However,
marketeers should still enjoy the
butterflies while they still last.
Focus on capitalising on the moment by
offering promotions or special deals
that encourage consumers to spend. The
key is to capture short-term value from
them without expecting long-term
loyalty. Now let's look at the true
friends. These are the best customers.
They are both profitable and loyal.
True friends bring a lot of business
and keep coming back, making them
incredibly valuable to your company.
These consumers are the lifeblood for
your business. True friends are more
than just loyal, they actively advocate
for your brand, share your positive
experiences, and often refer others to
your businesses. It's crucial to invest
in true friends by maintaining strong
relationships, offering personalised
experiences, and rewarding them with
their loyalty. The goal is to keep
these consumers happy and ensure they
stay loyal for the long run. They are
your brand ambassadors and they help
grow your consumer base through a
positive word of mouth. Finally, we
have Barnacles. These are consumers who
are highly loyal but not very
profitable. While they come back
regularly, they don't contribute much
to your bottom line. An example of
Bernacle is a small bank consumer who
frequently makes transactions with the
branch but doesn't maintain a large
enough balance to be profitable for
that. Barnacles can be most problematic
consumers because their loyalty is high,
but they don't drive much revenue.
Marketeers need to determine how to
maximise the profitability for
Barnacles, whether through selling
additional services, encouraging higher
spending, or finding other avenues to
convert them to true friends. In
conclusion, understanding these four
relation groups help businesses
allocate resources efficiently. Changes
require minimal effort. Butterflies
provide short-term value. True friends
are worth long-term investment, and
barnacles present challenges that need
to be managed strategically.
