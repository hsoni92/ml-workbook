Welcome to Module 11, Topic 4,
Explainability, audit trials, and
regulatory context. At some point,
someone will ask, Why did the model do
that? Maybe it's a user who was denied
a loan, a business stakeholder, or a
regulator. To answer that question, we
need to be able to trace how data
flowed through the system, which model
made the decision, and what influenced
that output. In this video, we'll see
how explainability and audit trials.
Work together
as an accountability toolkit for
production machine learning system.
Explainability is about making model
behaviour understandable enough that
human can reason about it. There are
two big flavours. Local explainability
asks why did we get this prediction for
this specific case.
Global explainability asks. In general,
what features or patterns drives this
model's behaviour? Explainability
supports debugging, building user trust
and satisfying regulatory or internal
review requirements. We don't need
perfect transparency. But we do need
useful, honest summaries of how the
model works. Let's separate local and
global explanations. A local
explanation. Is about a single case.
For example, for this loan application,
income and repayment history
contributed most to the decision.
These are useful for user facing
explanation, appeals, or support
tickets. A global explanation
summarises behaviour over many cases.
For example, across the data set,
credit history, debt to income ratio,
and recent delinquency are the top
drivers of risk. These are useful in
model reviews, feature design
and risk analysis. In production
machine learning, you usually need both
local explanation or specific decisions,
and global explanations to understand
and govern the system. Why do we invest
in explainability? First, debugging and
safety. Explanations can reveal strange
dependencies, like a model relying
heavily on postcode.
Which might act as a proxy for
something sensitive. Second, user trust
and experience. When people get a
decision, especially a negative one. A
clear, honest reason helps. It can also
guide them on what to improve or how to
appeal. Third, internal and regulatory
review. Stakeholders will ask.
What's really driving these decisions,
and is this aligned with our policies
and commitments? Explainability gives
you a way to answer. So explainability
is not just nice to have, it's part of
being a responsible system owner.
A quick warning. Explanations are not
magic truth machines. Many popular
explanability methods provide
approximations.
They simplify complex models into
something your brain or our brain can
handle,
which means they can oversimplify or
mislead. Explanations are also only as
good as the data
and features underneath. If data is
biassed, or features are proxies for
sensitive attributes, a pretty
explanation chart doesn't fix that.
There is a risk of telling just so
stories, explanations that sounds
plausible, but gloss over deeper
problems.
So use explainability alongside domain
expertise, fairness checks and sanity
checks, not as a substitute for them.
