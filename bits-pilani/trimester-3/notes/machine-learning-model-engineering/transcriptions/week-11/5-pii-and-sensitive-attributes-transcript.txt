Welcome to Module 11, Topic 2. Data
privacy in Machine Learning. Most real
machine learning systems run on
personal data. That means privacy is
not optional or nice to have. It's a
nonfunctional requirement, just like
latency or up time. In this video, we
will clarify what counts as personal
identifiable information and sensitive
attributes, talk about data
minimization and anonymisation.
And see how access control and
governance fits in. A helpful mental
model is, you are borrowing users' data,
and provide value, not owning it
outright.
We usually think about nonfunctional
requirements like latency, uptime, and
cost. Privacy sits right next to those.
For any machine learning system, you
should be able to answer, what data are
we collecting? How long do we keep it?
Who can access it, and for what purpose?
These answers save your features, data
pipeline, logging and monitoring. So
privacy is not something you bolt on
later. You consider it while you design
the system.
Let's clarify what we're protecting.
PII, or Personally Identifiable
Information.
Includes direct identifiers like names,
emails, phone numbers, government IDs,
and exact addresses. It also includes
quasi identifiers such as date of birth,
zip code, IP addresses, or device IDs,
things that might identify someone when
combinations.
We also have sensitive attributes
health data, financial information,
biometric, and often things like race,
religion, political views, or sexual
orientation. In machine learning, these
often show up as features or even
labels, which makes them powerful but
also high risk. They need spatial
handling.
PII is not just obvious fields labelled
name or email. It often hides inside
features. Sometimes we directly use
identifiers like user ID or email
driven features. Other times we use
very detailed location history,
transaction logs, or health
measurements. These may not look like
PII, but in practise they can be highly
identifying and sensitive.
Even a combination of harmless fields,
like very granular time stamps, plus
location, plus a few demographics, can
pinpoint individuals. As a model
engineer, keep asking.
Could this feature help identify a
person or expose something sensitive
about them? If the answer is yes, treat
it with extra care. Thank you.
