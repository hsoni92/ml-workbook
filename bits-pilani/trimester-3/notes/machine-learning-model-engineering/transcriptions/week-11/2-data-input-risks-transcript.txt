Why should we care about security for
machine learning specifically? First,
these models increasingly sit in the
decision loop for real things loan
approvals, fraud checks, content
moderations, healthcare triage, dynamic
pricing, and more. If they are attacked
or misused, the damage hits both users
and the business. Think unfair
decisions, privacy leaks, fraud, losses,
regulatory fines, and reputational
damage. Second, machine learning
systems are often harder to reason
about than rule-based logic.
That makes them Attractive targets.
Attackers can keep poking your model
with inputs and observe the outputs,
trying to find weaknesses. So security
becomes part of being a good model
engineer, not just an add-on for
someone else to figure out.
We will group threads into 4 buckets
first.
Data threat, poisoned or low quality
training data, skewed samples, or
corrupted labels that quietly change
what the model learns. Second. Input
threads, unusual or adversarial style
inputs. Out of distribution traffic, or
simple abuse patterns that push the
model outside its comfort zone. 3rd,
model and privacy threat situations
where an attacker tries to reconstruct
the model or extract sensitive
information from its output. 4th.
Infrastructure and access, unprotected
APIs, weak authentications, or exposed
locks. And feature stores, we will walk
through each bucket at high level,
focusing on what to watch for and how
to think defensively. Let's start with
data threat.
Data poisoning is the idea that someone
or some process manages to inject bad
or malicious data into your training
set. Over time, the model may learn
behaviours that benefit an attacker,
for example, approving certain
fraudulent pattern more often. Even
without a deliberate attack, you can
get label noise or inconsistent
labelling over time. Maybe different
team apply slightly different rules, or
a bug mislabel a portion of the data.
Your offline matrix looks fine, but the
model is learning from shaky ground.
Finally. skewed sample, where some
group, region, or behaviours are over
or underrepresented, can create blind
spots and fairness issues. As model
engineer, we don't need to be paranoid,
but we do need to assume that data
quality is a security and fairness
concern, not just an accuracy concern.
Next are input threats.
Problems at prediction time. Models
assume that incoming data looks similar
to what they saw during training, when
they receive out of distribution inputs,
strange formats, extreme values, or
completely new patterns.
Their predictions can become unreliable.
On top of that, there are adversary
style inputs.
At a high level, this is when someone
deliberately crafts inputs or sends a
large number of queries to explore your
model weak spots and decision
boundaries. Even simple abuse pattern
matters, an attacker might hammer your
API with many variations until they
find one that bypasses a fraud rule or
the content philtre. Detection, rate
limiting, input validation and
monitoring are unusual input patterns
are your friend here.
