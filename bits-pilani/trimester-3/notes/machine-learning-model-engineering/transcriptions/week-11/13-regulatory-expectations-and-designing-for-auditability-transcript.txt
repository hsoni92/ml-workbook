We won't go deep into specific laws.
But many domains, finance, healthcare,
hiring, and others share common
expectations. Typically, you are
expected to use data with a clear
purpose and appropriate consent.
Avoid unjustified discrimination or
harmful bias. Be able to explain
decisions in understandable terms to
non technical people.
Keep records of which model were used,
how they were evaluated and who
approved them.
When a user asks, why was I denied?
Or, a regulator asks, how is this model
governed?
You want explainability plus audit
trails ready to go. Remember, this is
not legal advice. The key takeaway for
you is to design for auditability and
accountability.
The best time to think about
auditability is at design time, not
after an incident. Treat model
versioning and logging as core
requirement, just like accuracy or
latency. Decide early what you need to
log, metrics, decisions, key features,
or even group or group wise metrics,
from the fairness works in Topic 3.
Make it easy to answer basic questions.
Which model made this decision? What
data and features were used?
What fairness, quality, or regression
checks did that model pass? Finally,
create simple playbook that describe
how to investigate a complaint or
incident. When something goes wrong,
you don't want to invent the process on
the spot.
