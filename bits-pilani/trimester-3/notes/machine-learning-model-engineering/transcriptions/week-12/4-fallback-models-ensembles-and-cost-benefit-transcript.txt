Another important routing pattern is
fallback models. Sometimes the main
model is uncertain, or you detect that
the input is out of distributions or
the main service is temporarily down.
In those situations, you can route to a
fallback model. The fallback might be a
simpler, more conversational model or
group based system, or even a manual
review, or deny by default path,
depending on domain.
The goal is not to be fancy, it's to
avoid catastrophic failures and
maintain predictable behaviour during
incidents or unusual inputs. Now let's
switch to ensembles. Instead of
choosing single model, we call multiple
models on the same input and then
combine their outputs. Three common
patterns. Averaging We average the
probability scores from different
models.
Each model votes for a class, and we
take the majority. Stacking, a separate
model, sometimes called a meta model,
learns how to combine the outputs of
several base models. Ensembles are
popular because they often give better
accuracy and robustness than any
individual model. And they reduce
sensitivity to one model's quirks or
bugs.
Ensemble sound great, but they come
with trade offs.
One on the plus side, they often
delivers higher accuracy and can be
more robust to noise or drift when the
models are diverse. On the minus side,
you are now running multiple models per
request, which increases compute cost,
latency, and operational complexity.
This brings us to the Module 8 picture
accuracy, latency, cost, and user
experience pulling in different
directions. Ensemble usually push up
accuracy, but also push up cost and
latency. As a model engineer. It's your
job to decide whether that tradeoff is
worth it for your particular use case.
A few practical tips. Start with simple
rule-based routing segment by country,
language, or product. You don't need a
fancy router model on day one.
Keep the number of models small
initially. Once you have a dozen model
variants, it becomes difficult to
understand and maintain the system.
Always measure per model and per
segment performance, so you know why
each route or assemble exists and what
value it provides, and document the
routing and ensemble logic, which rules
you use, which models are combined. And
how this relates to your SLOs and costs.
