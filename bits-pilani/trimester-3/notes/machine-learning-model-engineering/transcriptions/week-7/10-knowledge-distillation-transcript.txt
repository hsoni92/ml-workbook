The second technique is pruning. The
idea is to remove parts of the model
that don't contribute much of its
prediction.
There are two main styles. Unstructured
pruning zeroes out individual weights
based on some importance score.
That leads to very sparse matrix. But
you often need special kernels to
really benefit from the sparsity.
Structured pruning remove entire neuron,
philtres or channels. That changes the
shape of the layer but gives you a
smaller, dense network that standard
hardware can handle efficiently. In
most workflow, you prune weights
according to some creations.
Then fine tune the model for a new
epoch to regain some of the lost
accuracy. Pruning is especially useful
when you suspect that your model is
overparameterized.
The third technique is known as
distillation. Here, instead of just
shrinking the same model, we keep a big,
accurate teacher model, design a
smaller student model with fewer
parameters, and train the student model
to imitate the teacher's output. The
key idea is that the student doesn't
just see the Hard label, cat or dog? To
see the teacher's full probability
distribution over classes which
contains extra information about which
classes are similar. As a result,
students can end up much smaller and
faster, but often more accurate, and
another small model trained only on
hard labels. Distillation is very
popular when deploying to edge on
mobile devices. Thank you.
