The first big retraining trigger is
data drift and quality changes. You
might see strong drift scores on
important features, new segments or
categories that the model has never
seen before, or persistent data quality
issues like suddenly higher missing
value rates. These are warning signs,
but they are not an automatic retrain
now button.
The first step is always investigation.
Is this a legitimate business change?
Is it a data pipeline bug?
Is the model actually performing worse,
or just seeing different inputs? Once
you have confirmed that the model is
now operating on a meaningful,
different data regime. Retraining, with
newer data, becomes a stronger opinion.
The 2nd major trigger is performance
degradation on fresh label data.
Over time, you may observe low accuracy
or AUC.
Worse Root mean square error on
regression tasks.

Did the evaluation logic change? Did

We have ruled those out, and the

Not all retrains come from matrix
dashboard.
Some come from policy and product
decisions. Few examples, like new
regulation requires us to exclude
certain features or provide better
explanation.
A fairness review shows that a
particular group is underserved and you
decide and we decide to rebalance
training or change the loss function.
The product itself changes dramatically.
New pricing structure, new onboarding
funnel, new countries. These shifts can
make the old model structurally
misaligned.
With the new world, even if performance
metrics haven't completely tanked up.
In these cases, retraining is about
aligning with policy and product intent,
not just chasing slightly higher AUC.
