In microservice style architecture, the
model lives in its own separate service.
That service runs its own process or
container, exposes an API, often what
we say predict function over HTTP or
GRPC. And is called by other part of
the system. A typical flow might be
your front end, your back end, and a
model service, or a batch job, calling
model service to score data. The key
idea is that your model service has a
clear contract. A known request schema,
a known response schema, and often
specific SLOs for latency and up time.
It's no longer just a function inside a
big gap.
It becomes its own product like service
within your architecture.
Why go through the trouble of splitting
the model into its own service? First,
independent scaling. You can scale the
model service up or down separately
from the rest of your app.
If the model needs more CPUs, GPUs, or
memory, you adjust it replicas without
touching the front end.
Second,
TechStack Freedom, the model service
can run Python with specific ML
libraries, use GPUs, or have its own
dependencies. The rest of your system
might be in different language or
runtime, and that's fine.
Third, clear ownership and contracts. A
team can own the model API, its
Syschema, and its SLO. Other teams just
know here is how to call a predict
function. Here is what you get back.
Fourth, deployment and rollbacks are
easier. You can deploy a new model
version by updating just the model
service. If something goes wrong, you
can roll back that service without
touching everything else. These
benefits becomes more and more valuable
as the model becomes central and high
traffic.
Of course, microservices are not free.
Some of the trade-offs we have here as
well.
More deployment complexity. Now you
have more services to deploy, more
configurations to do, and more
dashboards to put up.
You need to be serious about API design
and versioning. On other team depends
on your predict API.
Changing it becomes really harder.
There is a network overhead.
Calls are no longer in process. They go
over HTTP or GRPC. This adds latency
potential failure points.
You need better monitoring and tracking.
When something goes wrong, you have to
trace requests across multiple services.
Microservice style serving is usually
the right choice when the model is
critical to the product, traffic is
high. You need to separate the scaling
for the model. And you expect to
iterate on the model frequently and
independently of the rest of the
application.
