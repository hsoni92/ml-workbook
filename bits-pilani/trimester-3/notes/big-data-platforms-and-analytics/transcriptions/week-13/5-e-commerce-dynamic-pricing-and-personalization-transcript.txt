Welcome back. While our previous video
focused on preventing loss in financial
services, this session explores how
stream processing is used to actively
drive revenue in e-commerce. We see a
powerful statistic, that is a 15%
average revenue lift through e-commerce
optimisation. Central to this is
dynamic pricing. In the modern
marketplace, prices are no longer
static. They respond instantly to
supply, demand, and competitor data.
There are 3 key examples of this in
action.
Surge pricing. Think of logistics
giants like Uber or Lyft. They use a
continuous stream of location data and
ride requests to adjust prices in real
time based on current demand, then
inventory-based discounts. Retailer can
trigger instant price drops if sensor
data shows stock isn't moving fast
enough in a specific region. Then user
intent scoring. We can actually issue
real-time coupons to a customer while
they are still on the site based on
their immediate browsing behaviour.
This leads us to real-time
personalization, often referred to as
the Amazon effect. The goal here is to
match user intent while they are still
browsing. For a recommendation engine
to truly influence a purchase decision,
it cannot be based on what a user liked
last week. It must be near instant. To
achieve this, we rely on contextual
awareness. This involves combining a
user's current browsing data, the
products they're looking at right now,
with their historical preferences
stored in that global state we
discussed earlier. By merging current
events with historical context, the
streaming engine creates a unique
personalised experience for every user.
Updated every single second.
As a data scientist, your goal is to
architect systems that move past
generic, top sellers lists and towards
truly individualised real-time
responses.
We have seen how streaming logic solves
business problems in finance and retail.
But what is the actual backbone that
support these flows? In the next lesson,
we will shift our focus to the
architectural tools of the trade,
