Welcome to Lesson 4. We have spent the
previous lessons discussing the why and
what of stream processing. Now we are
moving into the how. In this lesson, we
look at the architectures that make
real time flows possible.
Starting with the industry standard,
that is Apache Kafka. If you look at
the slide, Kafka is described as the
streaming backbone. In a modern data
ecosystem, you have dozens of different
data sources like web logs, IoT sensors,
or databases. And dozens of different
destinations that need that data.
Without a backbone, you end up with a
spaghetti of connections.
Kafka solves this by acting as a
persistent, fault tolerant log of
events that sits directly between your
producers and your consumers.
Let's break down the architecture shown
in the diagram on the slide. Producers,
these are the applications pushing data
into the system. This could be anything
from a mobile app sending click stream
events to a thermometer in a shipping
container sending temperature readings.
Kafka cluster or brokers, the heart of
the system. Kafka doesn't just pass
messages. It stores them in a highly
scalable cluster of brokers.
Because these messages are written to
disc and replicated. Kafka provides the
durability we need.
If a processing engine fails, the data
is still safe in the Kafka broker.
Consumers, these are the systems
reading the data to do the actual work.
This could be a spark streaming job, a
database, or, as we'll discuss in our
next video, an Apache storm topology.
The beauty of this architecture is that
it decouples your sources from your
targets. Producers don't need to know
who is consuming.
And consumers can read the data at
their own pace, without worrying about
producers pushing the data into brokers.
This decoupling is what allows us to
scale real-time intelligence up to
millions of events per second.
Kafka is where the data lives while
it's in the motion, but how do we
actually transform that data? In the
next video, we'll look at the Apache
storm and how it uses spouts and bolts
to process these Kafka streams in real
time.
