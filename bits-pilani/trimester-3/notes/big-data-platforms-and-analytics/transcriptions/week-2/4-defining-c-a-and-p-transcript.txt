Welcome back. In our last lesson, we
learned that in a distributed system,
network partitions, those breaks in
communication, are a guaranteed reality.
Today we are going to learn the golden
rule of how we deal with those breaks.
It's called the Cap theorem. Proposed
by Eric Brewer. Cap theorem is a
fundamental law that states in a
distributed system, you can only
provide 2 out of 3 guarantees at any
given time. Those 3 guarantees are
consistency, availability, and
partition tolerance. Before we look at
the trade-off, we first need to
understand exactly what each of these
letters means in the real world.
First, let's talk about consistency,
see? In a consistent system, every read
request receives the most recent write.
Or an error. Think of it like a group
text with your friends. If you send a
message saying
lunch is at 1:00 p.m. and your friend
checks the group text a second later, a
consistent system guarantees that they
see your message about 1:00 p.m. first.
They won't see an old message about
12:00 p.m. It's about everyone seeing
the exact same truth at the exact same
time. If the system can't guarantee
that the data is the most recent
version, it would rather return an
error than give you the wrong answer.
Next is availability, or A. This means
that every request receives a response,
without the guarantee that it contains
the most recent right.
Imagine you're trying to check the
score of a cricket match on a news
website. An available system will
always give you a score when you
refresh the page. Even if the server in
your city has lost its connection to
the stadium. And is showing a score
from 5 minutes ago, the side stays up.
It's better to give some answer than no
answer. In an available system, up time
is the most important thing. Finally,
we have partition tolerance. As we
learned in Lesson 2. That it is the
system's ability to keep working even
when messages are being dropped or
delayed between nodes. Since we are
working with big data clusters spread
across many machines, P is a
requirement. We know the network will
eventually fail. So the real question
the cap theorem asks is, when a
partition or a break happens, do you
choose to be consistent, which means
give the perfect truth or nothing. Or
available, which means, give whatever
answer you have, so the user isn't
stuck.
In order to recap, consistency is about
being correct, availability is about
being fast, and partition tolerance is
about being resilient. Because we
cannot avoid partitions, we are forced
to choose between C and A. In our next
lesson, we will talk at real world
scenarios like banking versus social
media to see which choice is the right
one for different business problems.
