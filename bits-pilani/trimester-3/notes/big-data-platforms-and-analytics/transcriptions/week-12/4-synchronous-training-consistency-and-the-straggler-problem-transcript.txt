Welcome to Lesson 3. In the previous
lesson, we talked about where the model
weights live. Now we need to discuss
when they get updated. This brings us
to the concept of training modes. Let's
start with the synchronous training. In
a synchronous setup, the entire cluster
operates like a well drilled marching
band. The key rule is weight all. This
means every single worker in your
cluster must finish computing its
specific batch and calculate its
gradients before the model weights can
be updated. So only after everyone is
done do we aggregate these gradients
and move to the next step. The primary
benefit of this approach is consistency,
because every worker waits for the
update to finish before starting the
next batch. You are guaranteed that
every worker is always using the exact
same version of the model for every
training step. This makes the training
process mathematically identical to
training on a single machine, just much
faster. However, there is a major
downside often referred to as a
straggler problem. Imagine you have 10
high-speed GPUs working together, but
one of them is slightly slower. Perhaps
due to minor hardware issues, or due to
a network spike, or. Just
a different background task running on
that node, right? So because of the
weight all rule, those 9 other GPUs
which are fast will be sitting idle,
wasting expensive compute time while
they wait for that 1 straggler to
finish. In a synchronous world, your
cluster is only as fast as the slowest
member, as we might have discussed in
earlier videos as well. Despite the
risk, synchronous training is the most
common choice for many deep learning
task.
Why? Because it provides more stable
convergence.
In our next video, we'll look at the
asynchronous training, which tries to
solve the straggler problem but
introduces some interesting trade-offs
of its own.
