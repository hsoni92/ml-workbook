We have reached the end of Module 4.
Over the course of this module. We have
charted the evolution of batch
processing, moving from foundational
reliability of Hadoop to the high
performance speed of an Apache Spark.
We began with a deep dive into SDFS.
Where we learned that Hadoop
prioritises data durability through its
block and replicate strategy.
By breaking data into large blocks and
distributing 3 copies across different
tracks. HDFS ensures that even
significant hardware failures. Won't
result in data loss. However, we also
identify the Achilles' heel of the
Hadoop MapReduce model, high IO latency.
The requirement to write intermediate
results to a physical disc.
What we call materialisation cost.
Which creates a significant bottleneck,
especially for iterative workloads like
machine learning.
This led us to the in memory revolution
brought about by Apache Spark. By
utilising RAM as its. Primary workspace
and organising computations into an
intelligent directed a cyclic graph,
which is also called DAG. Spark
minimises unnecessarily discrete rights.
We saw through our Kamins benchmark
that this architectural shift can
result in performance gains up to 100
times
for certain algorithms. Finally, we
discussed the trade-offs. Hadoop
remains a cost effective solution for
massive scale archival storage on
commodity discs, while Spark offers
unparalleled speed at a higher hardware
cost due to its heavy reliance on RAM.
We should now be able to evaluate.
Which architecture best suits your
specific big data needs, based on
performance requirements and budget
constraints? Thank you for joining us
in module 4. I'll see you in the next
module. Till then, happy learning.
