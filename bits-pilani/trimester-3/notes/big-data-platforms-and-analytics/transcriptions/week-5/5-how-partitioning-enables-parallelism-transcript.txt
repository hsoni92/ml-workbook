These are the atomic units of
parallelism. Here is the rule of thumb.
One partition is typically process by
one CPU.
By breaking the data into these chunks,
Spark ensures that no node in your
cluster is sitting idle.
Let's say if you have 100 cores
available.
You want at least 100 partitions, so
every core has work to do.
This balance is what we call
parallelism.
To really see the impact of this, let's
look at the next slide, which is a
chart.
Look at the top bar. If we try to
process a massive dataset using just a

of 10 hours. The CPU is doing all the
work alone.
Now look at the middle bar. By using
Spark and dividing that same data into
just 10 partitions.
We can spread the work across 10
different courses.
Suddenly, that 10 hour job is finished
in just 1 hour, all the 10 cores
working parallelel to achieve the same
task in union.
But it gets even better if you scale up
to 100 partitions across a larger
cluster. That same task drops down to
just 6 minutes.
This is the core value proposition of
Spark, which is linear scalability. By
adding more partitions and more cores,
we can shrink processing times from
hours to minutes. Partitioning is what
