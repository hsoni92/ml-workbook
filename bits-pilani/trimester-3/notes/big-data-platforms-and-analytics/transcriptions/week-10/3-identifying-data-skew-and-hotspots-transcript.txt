Data skew is a condition where a small
number of data partitions are
significantly larger than the rest of
them. Because Spark and other
distributed engines process data one
partition at a time per core, an
oversized partition means one worker is
left doing a mountain of work while the
others finish their small hills and go
home.
So this imbalance is a silent killer of
your big data performance. The first
symptom to watch for is what we call
hotspot. When you monitor your
cluster's resource usage, perhaps
through a tool like Ganglia or
Cloudwatch, look for unevenness.
If you see specific nodes hitting 100%
CPUs or maxing out their memory, while
other nodes in the same cluster are
sitting at 5% or 10% or 15%, you are
sure that you have a hotspot. In a
perfectly healthy job, all your CPU
graphs should move together like a
synchronised heartbeat. When you see
one spike that stays high while the
others drop off, that node is
struggling with a skewed partition. And
the second and the most common symptom
is tail latency. Have you ever run a
spark job where progress bar shoots up
to 99% in 2 minutes but then stays at
the last 1% for another 10 minutes?
That is tail latency. To confirm this,
you need to dive into the spark UI.
Look at the summary metrics for your
stages. Pay close attention to the max
task versus the median task time.
In a balanced job, the max and the
medium should be relatively close.
However, if your median task is 2
seconds, but your max task is, let's
say 5 minutes. You have found your
straggler task. This single task is
holding up the entire stage. And it's
almost always caused by data skew. So
just to recap, look at the synchronised
heartbeat in your CPU logs to avoid
hotspot and check the max versus median
times in the Spark UI to catch
