We have covered a tremendous amount of
ground in this module, from the
fundamental challenges of networking to
the specific mechanics of distributed
optimisation. We have explored what it
truly takes to scale machine learning
beyond a single node. In many large
scale clusters, up to 70% of the total
training time is spent on communication
rather than computation. This is a
staggering statistic. It reminds us
that in the world of big data, training
time is dominated by data movement.
Rather than the underlying math. You
can have the fastest GPUs in the world,
but if your network is congested, those
GPUs will spend most of the time
waiting.
As we wrap up, let's revisit our three
most important takeaways. First,
Remember that scaling is limited by the
network, not just the GPU. When
designing a distributed system, you
must think about bandwidth, latency,
and synchronisation just as much as you
think about flops or memory capacity. A
balanced system is always more
efficient than a system with fast
compute and slow interconnects. Second
is data parallelism. It remains the
standard for most use cases. It is the
most robust and straightforward way to
scale. By sharding your data set and
keeping a full copy of the model on
every worker, you can effectively
utilise massive data sets to train high
performing models in a fraction of time.
It would take on a single machine.
Finally, we explored how model
parallelism enables the training of
multi-billion parameter networks.
Without strategies like pipeline and
tensor parallelism, the revolutionary
Lar Language models, or LLMs we see
today simply wouldn't be possible.
These techniques allow us to break
through the memory wall of a single
device by treating a cluster of GPUs as
one massive unified memory pool.
You're now equipped with the conceptual
framework needed to understand how
modern AI is built at scale, whether
you are managing a small cluster or
architecting a massive cloud-based
pipeline. These principles of
communication, parallelism, and
aggregation will be your guide. This
