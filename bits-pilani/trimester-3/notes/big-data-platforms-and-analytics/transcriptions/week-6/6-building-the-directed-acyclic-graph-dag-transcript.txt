write your Python or Scala code, Spark
doesn't see lines of text. It parses

of operations. This is essentially a
mathematical representation of your
logic. At this stage, Spark knows what
you want to do, but it hasn't decided
how to do it yet. It just records the
lineage, the sequence of
transformations, right, which are
required to produce a final result.
Next, Spark converts that logical plan
into a physical plan. This is where the
Catalyst optimizer steps in. Spark
evaluates several different strategies
to run your logic. For instance, it
might look at two different ways to
join your tables and choose the one
that minimises data movement across the
cluster. The output of this step is a
specific blueprint for execution
detailing which algorithms will be used
and how data will be accessed. Once the
physical plan is set, the DAG scheduler
breaks the graph into stages.
This is where our knowledge of
dependencies from Lesson 3 comes into
play. The scheduler looks for shuffle

between two shuffles is grouped into a

pipeline narrow transformations
together, ensuring that data stays in
memory as long as possible before it
has to be moved across the network. And
then finally we reach the task launch.
The stages are further divided into
individual tasks, which are the
smallest unit of work in Spark. Each
task corresponds to a single partition
of data. And a set of transformations.
These serialised tasks are then shipped
out to the executors across the cluster
for a parallel process. The driver
monitors these tasks, and once they all
are complete, your action finally
returns its results. By building the DA
in this structured way, Spark ensures
