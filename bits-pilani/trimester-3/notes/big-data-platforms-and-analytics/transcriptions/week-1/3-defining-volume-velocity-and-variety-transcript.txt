Welcome back, everyone. In our first
lesson, we talked about how the world
is shifting from bigger boxes to more
boxes. But before we start building
those clusters, we need to understand
why we are doing it in the first place.
What makes big data actually big? It's
not just about the size. It's about 3
specific challenges that we call the
three V's volume, velocity, and variety.
Today we're going to break these down
using simple words and see how they
show up in the real world of business.
First up is volume. In simple terms,
this is just the sheer amount of data.
Think back 10 years. A 16 GB phone felt
like it could hold your whole life.
Today we are dealing with data so
massive that it doesn't just fill up
your phone, it fills up entire
buildings full of servers. In big data,
we stopped talking about megabytes and
gigabytes and started talking about
terabytes, petabytes, and exabytes.
Let's discuss a business use case of
e-commerce retail. Imagine a giant like
Amazon or Walmart. Every single second,
millions of people are clicking on
products, adding items to carts, and
searching for reviews. If you recorded
every single click and every single
transaction from every customer
globally.
You'd have a volume problem. You can't
store the data on one computer. You
need a platform that can spread that
data across thousands of machines. The
second V is velocity. This isn't about
how much data you have. It's about how
fast the data is coming at you. Imagine
trying to take a sip of water from a
regular glass. That is traditional data.
Now imagine you're trying to take a sip
from a high pressure fire hose. That's
big data velocity. It's data in motion.
Let's discuss a business use case of
financial services or banking. Think
about credit card fraud detection when
you swipe your card at a coffee shop.
The bank has about 20 milliseconds,
less than the blink of an eye. To
decide if that transaction is
legitimate or if a hacker stole your
card. The data is flying in from
millions of card readers simultaneously.
If the bank waits an hour to process
their data in a batch, the thief is
already gone. They need to process that
data at a high velocity in real time.
The final V is variety. In the old days,
data was neat. It lived in rows and
columns in an Excel sheet. We call this
structured data, but today, Only 20% of
that data is neat. The other 80% is
unstructured. It is messy stuff, emails,
PDFs, satellite images, heart rate
monitor readings, and even voice notes.
Let's discuss the business use case of
modern healthcare. Think about a smart
hospital
for a single patient. The hospital has
structured data, which is their age,
their name, their billing info, and
then they have semi-structured data,
their digital prescriptions or lab
reports. Then they have their
unstructured data, the X-ray images,
MRI videos, and the doctor's
handwritten notes. To truly understand
a patient's health, a big data platform
has to be able to make sense of all
these three different varieties at once.
