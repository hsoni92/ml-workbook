In our last video, we looked at the
architecture of a cluster. The master
and the worker nods. But to truly
understand why the industry shifted to
this model, we have to look at the why
from a business perspective. Why do
companies like Google or Amazon choose
thousands of standard PCs over a single
high-end supercomputer? The answer lies
in the economics of commodity hardware.
Today we are going to look at how
standardised infrastructure provides
the cost efficiency and resilience
required to scale indefinitely.
The most important metric in the big
data isn't just a raw speed, it's
performance per dollar. As we discussed
in our vertical scaling lesson. High
end specialised hardware follows an
exponential cost curve doubling your
power on a single machine might
quadruple your price. However, With
commodity hardware, the cost is linear.
If you need twice the storage or twice
the compute, you simply buy twice the
number of standard nodes. Because
These are off the shelf components.
You're getting the best possible price
for every processing power. This allows
a startup to start small and grow their
budget only as their data grows,
avoiding upfront supercomputer
investment.
In a traditional data centre with
proprietary machines, if a specialised
part breaks, the cost of downtime is
massive. While you wait for a custom
repair in a commodity cluster, we treat
the hardware as disposable.
Because every node is standard unit, we
can achieve rapid node replacement. If
a worker node fails, a technician
doesn't spend hours troubleshooting a
motherboard. They simply pull the unit
out and slide a new identical commodity
server in its place.
This standardisation reduces the need
for specialised engineers, and keeps
the operational expense of the data
centres significantly lower. Finally,
we have the economic benefit of self
healing resilience. Because commodity
hardware is cheaper, we can afford to
be redundant. We typically replicate
our data 3 times. To do this on a 1
million supercomputer would cost $3
million which is impossible for most
businesses. But on commodity hardware,
replicating data across 3 $1000 nodes
is highly affordable. We are
essentially using smart software to
turn unreliable, cheap hardware into a
highly reliable and expensive service.
I hope you were able to gain some
insights into the economics of
commodity hardware.
