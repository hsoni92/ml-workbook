Welcome to Lesson 5. So far, we have
looked at the 2 off the shelf solutions
provided by most big data frameworks.
These are hash partitioning and range
partitioning.
These are fantastic for about 80% of
the use cases. But as we saw at the end
of the last lesson.
Standard strategies can struggle when
data becomes highly skewed, or follows
a complex nonlinear distribution.
We are entering the territory of custom
partitioners, this is where we move
from using general purpose math to
designing domain specific structures.
This is about writing logic that
understand the nature of your data.
Rather than just treating it as a
random string or a simple range.
What exactly do we mean by custom logic?
When the default partitioners fail to
distribute your data evenly, framework
like Spark allows you to define a
partitioner class or a custom function.
This function takes a key as an input
and returns a specific integer
representing the target position.
Essentially, you're taking the wheel,
instead of letting a modulo operator
decide where a record goes. You can
write NFL statement or a complex
algorithm to decide exactly which node
should process which piece of
information. We have highlighted two
prime examples of when you might need
this.
Geographical coordinates. Imagine you
are processing delivery data. Standard
hashing might put two neighbouring
street addresses on completely
different nodes, but if your goal is to
calculate local traffic patterns, you
need these neighbours on the same node,
right? So a custom partitioner.
Can group data based on map tiles or
geo hashes to ensure spatial locality.
The other is hierarchical business IDs.
If your company uses IDs where the
first three digits represent a country
or a specific department, a custom
practitioner can ensure that all
records for Department A stay together,
regardless of the unique ID that
follows.
The takeaway here is that you aren't
locked into the defaults. If your data
has a specific shape that computer
doesn't automatically understand, a
custom partitioner is your tool for
fixing it. Now that we understand the
theory and why behind custom logic, it
is time to see it in action.
In our next video, we're going to walk
through a code demo to show you exactly
how to build a custom partitioner in
Pie Park. I'll see you there. Till then,
happy learning and thank you so much.
