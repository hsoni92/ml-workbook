storage layer of the Hadoop ecosystem,
the HDFS, or the Hadoop Distributed
File System. To understand why Hadoop
behaves the way it does. We have to
look at how it physically handles your

We encounter the fundamental unit of
HDFS storage, the block. Unlike a
traditional PC file system that might
use a 4 KB or 8 KB blocks, HDFS is
designed for big data, meaning it uses
massive blocks, sizes, typically 128 MB
or even 256 MB. Why so large? It's all
about reducing seek time.
By making the blocks large, the time it
takes to transfer the data from disc
becomes significantly longer than the
time it took to find the start of the
block. This optimises the system for
high throughput sequential reads, which
is perfect for batch processing. Now
let's move to the next slide.
storing data in a large block is
efficient, but it's also risky. In a
cluster of thousands of commodity
servers, hardware failure isn't a
possibility. It's a mathematical
certainty. This brings us to the slide
and the SDFS replication strategy. By
default, HDFS follows a 3x replication
rule. When you upload a file, HDFS
doesn't just store one copy to each
block, it creates 3. But it's the
placement of these copies that makes
SDFS so resilient. SDFS is rack aware.
Typically, the 1st copy stays on the
local node. The 2nd copy is sent to a
different node on a completely
different rack. The 3rd copy is placed
on another node with the same 2nd rack.
This strategy provides a perfect
balance. If a single disc fails, you
have two other copies. If an entire
rack loses power or its still network
switch fails, you still have a copy of
your data on a different rack
altogether.
This block and replicate model is the
bedrock of Hadoop's reliability,
ensuring that even in the face of
hardware chaos, your data remains safe
and accessible.
