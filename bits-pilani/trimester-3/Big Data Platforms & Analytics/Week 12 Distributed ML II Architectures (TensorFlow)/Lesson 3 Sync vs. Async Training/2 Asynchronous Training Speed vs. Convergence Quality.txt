In our last video we saw how a single
straggler. Can slow down an entirely
synchronous cluster. To address this,
we use asynchronous training. In this
mode, we throw away the weight all rule.
Instead, every worker updates the
global weights independently as soon as
it finishes its specific batch. If one
worker is faster than others, it simply
keeps going. Pulling the latest weights,
pushing new gradients without ever
waiting for its peers.
The primary advantage here is 100%
worker utilisation. You're getting the
absolute maximum out of your hardware
because no GPU ever sits idle. This
makes asynchronous training incredibly
fast in terms of raw steps per second.
However, there is a significant
trade-off in the convergence quality
because workers aren't synced. A worker
might calculate gradients using an
older version of the weights, while
other workers have already updated the
master model.
So this is known as a stale gradient
problem.
Using these stale updates can lead to
instability, making it harder for the
model to reach its peak accuracy, or
requiring more careful tuning of the
learning rate.
So to wrap up this lesson, let's look
at how these two modes actually perform
in the real world based on standard
cluster benchmarks. So if you look at
speed measured in steps per second,
asynchronous training is a clear winner,
often reaching 95% efficiency or higher.
Then synchronous training typically
lags behind around 70% in this example.
Because of that one straggler overhead
that we just discussed, but when we
look at final accuracy, the story flips.
Synchronous training usually achieves
higher accuracy, you know, 98% here in
this example, because every update is
mathematically fresh and consistent.
Asynchronous training might stop at a
lower accuracy or take much longer to
converge. To that same peak because of
the noise introduced by those stale
gradients. The choice between them
often comes down to your hardware. If
you have a very stable high speed
network, synchronous is usually best.
If you're working with unreliable nodes
or varying hardware speeds,
asynchronous might be your only path to
finishing the job.
