In our last video, we saw how vertical
scaling, that means buying a bigger
truck, can only take you so far before
it becomes too expensive or physically
impossible. Today we are going to look
at the architectural revolution that
made Google, Amazon, and Netflix
possible, and that is horizontal
scaling, also known as scaling out.
Remember our delivery driver analogy?
If vertical scaling was buying a bigger
and bigger truck, horizontal scaling is
like hiring 10 more drivers with 10
smaller vans. You aren't making one
vehicle stronger, you are making the
system larger by adding more units. In
the tech world, this means adding more
servers, what we call nodes, into a
single group called a cluster. Let's
look at a business problem that a
company like Netflix faces. On a Friday
night, millions of people around the
world decide to watch a new movie at
the exact same time. No single computer
in the world, no matter how much RAM
you have shoved into it, can stream
high definition video to 200 million
people simultaneously. This pipe is
just too small. The solution?
Distribute the load. Instead of one
super server in California trying to
talk to the whole world, Netflix uses
thousands of standard, smaller servers
spread across the globe. When you hit
play, you aren't talking to the main
brain of Netflix. You're talking to the
nearest van in the fleet, that is
closest to your house. This is the
power of horizontal scaling. It allows
you to handle nearly infinite growth by
just adding more vans to the fleet. One
of the biggest advantage for a business
here is the cost. In video one we
mentioned the price wall where high-end
RAM becomes exponentially expensive.
Horizontal scaling uses what we call
commodity hardware. These are standard
off the shelf servers, the kind you
find in any data centre. They aren't
special because they are standard.
They're cheap. If you need more power,
you don't buy a $50,000 upgrade for one
machine. You buy 5 more $2000 machines.
This makes the cost of growth linear.
If your data troubles or it becomes
thrice or twice, your cost roughly
doubles, it doesn't explode. This is
how startups can afford to grow into
giants. Finally, let's talk about
business continuity. What happens if a
van breaks down? In a vertical system,
if one big truck breaks, the business
stops. But in a horizontal system, if
one server in a cluster of 100 fails,
you still have 99 servers running. This
is called fault tolerance. In big data
platforms like Hadoop or Spark, the
software is smart enough to see that
node A has died, and it automamatically
moves node A's work to node B. The
customer watching the movie never even
notices a glitch. However, there is a
tax for this complexity. Now you have
to manage a network, and you have to
make sure all those servers can talk to
each other without getting confused. In
our next video, we will put vertical
and horizontal scaling side by side to
help you decide which one to choose for
your specific business problem. I'll
see you there. Till then, happy
learning.
