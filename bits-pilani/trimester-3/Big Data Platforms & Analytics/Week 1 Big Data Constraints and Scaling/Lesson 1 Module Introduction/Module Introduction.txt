Hey everyone. Welcome to the first
module, Big Data Constraints and
scaling for Week One. Before we dive
deep into complex tools like Spark or
Hadoop later in this course, we need to
answer a very basic question, yet an
important question. Why can't we just
use a regular computer for big data?
Most of us are used to solving problems
by getting a faster device or adding
more RAM, but when we talk about big
data, we aren't just talking about a
large file. We're talking about the
data that is growing so fast and in so
many different formats that it actually
evolves the way traditional computers
work.
In this module, our first stop is
understanding the three V's volume,
velocity, and variety. Volume is about
size. Imagine trying to fit the entire
ocean into a single bucket. Velocity is
about speed, data coming at you like a
fire hose that never turns off. Variety
is about types, mixing photos, text,
sensor data, videos, all at once. We
will explore how these three factors
create constraints or walls that force
us to rethink how we build systems.
Next, we will look at the physical
limits of a single machine. We often
think that if we just buy a more
expensive supercomputer, we can solve
any problem. This is called vertical
scaling or scaling up. However, we will
learn why this approach eventually
fails. Not only does it become
incredibly expensive, but physics
eventually halts the way. Your CPU's
speed will have a logical limit, and
your storage can only fetch data up to
a limit. And Then they hit a hard
ceiling. When you hit that ceiling, the
system crashes, your business stops.
This brings us to the core theme of
this module, horizontal scaling or
scaling out. Instead of buying one
giant expensive mega machine, what if
we use hundreds of regular cheap
computers, what we call commodity
hardware, and make them work together
in union. This shift from bigger boxes
to more boxes is the foundation of
every big data platform you will use in
this course. It is the difference
between one giant person trying to lift
a mountain and a million people each
carrying a single stone. We're setting
the foundation today so that when we
start coding later, you'll understand
the why behind the how. Happy learning.
