Welcome to lesson 3. So far we have
talked about the wall in the hardware.
Now we will discuss about why bigger
boxes eventually fail. In the world of
hardware, the relationship between
power and price is not a straight line.
It's a curve. If a standard server
costs $5000 a server that is twice as
powerful might cost $25,000. A server
that is four times as powerful might
cost $150,000.
Cloud providers, why do companies like
AWS or Google Cloud use millions of
cheap servers instead of one giant
supercomputer? Because it is cheaper.
It is much more cost effective to buy
100 normal computers than one monster
computer, and that has the same total
power. We call this moving from
specialised hardware to commodity
hardware. The biggest risk with a
bigger box is that it is a single point
of failure. If you have one massive,
expensive server running your entire
company's database and a single power
supply. Fails or single motherboard
short circuits, your whole business
goes offline. You're all in one machine.
Imagine an airline like Delta or
Emirates. If they ran their booking
system on one massive bigger box and it
crashed during a holiday weekend,
thousands of flights would be grounded.
In big data, we prefer a cluster. If 1
machine out of 1000 fails, the other
999 just keep working. The system is
resilient. Finally, bigger boxes have a
ceiling. You can only add so many CPUs
to a single motherboard before you run
out of slots. When you reach that
ceiling, you're stuck. You have to
throw away the whole bigger machine and
buy an even bigger one. But with
horizontal scaling, which is like
adding more small machines, there is no
ceiling. If your data grows, you just
buy another locker and add it to the
network. Now that you have good
understanding of why bigger boxes
eventually fail, let's dig deeper into
the concepts in our subsequent lessons.
Happy learning till then. Thank you.
