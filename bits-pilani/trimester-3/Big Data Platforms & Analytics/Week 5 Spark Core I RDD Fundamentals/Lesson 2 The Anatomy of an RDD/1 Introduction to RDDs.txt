Now that we have had a bird's eye view
of our goals for this module, let's
dive into the most important concept in
Spark. That is the RDD. If you look at
the slide, you'll see that RDD stands
for Resilient Distributed Dataset. To
truly understand Spark, you need to
understand what each of these three
words means. Let's break it down. The R
is for resilient. In the world of big
data, we are often working with
hundreds or even thousands of computers.
In a system that large, something is
bound to break eventually. A hard drive
might fail or a network cable might
come loose. Resilient means that Spark
is fault tolerant. If a piece of data
is lost because a computer fails, Spark
doesn't lose that data forever. It has
a recipe to automatically rebuild that
data and keep the job running. It's
self-healing.
Then the next D is for distributed.
When we say data is distributed, we
mean it isn't just sitting on one
single hard drive. Instead, Spark takes
your massive data set and spreads it
out across the memory of many different
computers in a cluster. Even though
it's spread out physically. Spark
allows you to treat it as one single
unit.
This is what allows us to process
terabytes of data in minutes rather
than days. And then the next D is for
dataset. This is the simplest part.
At its core, an RDD is just a
collection of objects. It could be a
list of numbers, rows of text from a
log file, or complex customer records.
It's the actual stuff you're working
with. So when you put it all together,
an RDD is a collection of data that is
spread across many computers and is
smart enough to fix itself if something
goes wrong. In our next video, we're
going to look closer at the resilient
part to understand exactly how Spark
manages to never lose its place. I'll
see you there. Thank you so much. Happy
