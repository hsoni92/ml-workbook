So in our last video, we identify
shuffle as a major bottleneck due to
slow network IO. But what if we could
eliminate the shuffle entirely?
When you are performing an asymmetric
joint, meaning you have one very large
table and one relatively small table,
You have a golden opportunity to use a
broadcast joint. Instead of moving both
data sets across the network to meet in
the middle, we simply send a complete
copy of the small table to every single
node.
Let's compare the two diagrams on the
slide. Look at the shuffle join side
first. Notice the crisscrossing arrows.
Both table A and table B are being
sliced up and sent across the network.
This involves heavy visualisation and
massive network traffic. Now look at
the broadcast joint side. There is no
all to all communication. Instead, the
small table is broadcasted to every
node.
Once a small table is sitting in the
local ram of every worker, the joint
happens locally. The big table doesn't
have to move an inch. It stays right
where it was partitioned, and the join
is performed at memory speeds rather
than the network speeds.
This sounds like a magic, but there are
specific conditions you must meet.
As the slides indicate, you should use
this when one table is small. In Spark,
the default threshold is often around
10 megabytes, though you can tune this,
right? You can increase this limit as
well. The primary constraint is that
the small table must fit entirely
within the executor's RAM. If you try
to broadcast a table that is too large,
you will trigger an out of memory error.
However, when used correctly for things
like lookup of product names, category
codes, or user metadata against a
massive stream of transactions, the
performance gains are massive. So
you're effectively trading a small
amount of memory on each node to gain a
huge reduction in the network traffic
and execution time. By mastering
broadcast joins, you can turn a stage.
That used to take minutes into one that
takes seconds. It is one of the most
effective quick wins in big data
optimisation. But what if both tables
