Welcome to Lesson 3. In the previous
lessons, we established that Spark
actually relies on recomputation rather
than replication. But how does Spark
know exactly what to recomput? It uses
a logical map, which is called RDD
lineage graph. Think of lineage graph
as the brain of sparks fault tolerance
system. It's a directed a cyclic graph
or dag. Which keeps track of every
transformation applied to your data. It
doesn't store the data itself, but it
stores the history of how the data was
built. If a node fails and the piece of
data goes missing, Spark doesn't panic.
It simply looks at the road map,
identifies the missing piece, and
follows the path backward to the last
known good state to recreate it. To
understand the efficiency of this road
map. We can look at it through a simple
mathematical lens. On this slide You
see the equation for a recovery cost.
The cost to recover a lost partition is
essentially the sum of the time it
takes to execute each operation in that
partition's history, starting from the
last checkpoint or the source data.
In this formula, N actually represents
the number of steps, or the
transformations in the linear change.
And output of i represent the each
individual operation.
This formula tells us something
critical. Spark's recovery time is
proportional to the depth of the graph.
If your lineage is short. Say just a
simple philtre on a text file, the
recovery is nearly instant, but if your
lineage is very long and complex. The
recovery cost actually increases.
This is why understanding the anatomy
of your graph is vital for building
high performance resilient applications.
So what makes this road map so reliable?
It stands on 3 core architectural
pillars. First is immutability. Which
is, RDDs cannot change. Once an RDD is
created, it is a fixed state. Because
it is read-only snapshot, we don't have
to worry about data changing midstream,
right? Each transformation produces a
brand new fixed state. Second is
ancestry. Every RDD in Spark maintains
a pointer or a reference to its parent
RDD.
And this creates a chain of custody.
Because RDDB knows it came from RDDA.
It knows exactly who to ask for data if
its own partitions are lost, right?
Finally, we have determinism.
This is the secret sauce. In Spark,
rerunning the same code on the same
input data is guaranteed to always
yield the exact same result. Without
determinism, the lineage of the recipe
would be useless because re-executing
it might give us different data than
what we lost.
So together, immutability, ancestry,
and determinism, they all three ensure
that your data is always recoverable,
no matter how large your cluster or how
frequent your hardware failures.
