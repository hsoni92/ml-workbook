Welcome to Lesson 5. In our previous
lessons, we looked at the 4-step cycle
of a distributed gradient update.
But there is a massive operational
question we haven't answered yet.
How strictly do we enforce the timing
of these updates? In a distributed
cluster, not every worker finishes
their math at the same microsecond or
nanosecond. How we handle those timing
differences, what we call our
aggregation strategy. It can change
everything from the speed of our
training to the final accuracy of the
model. Let's break down the 3 main
strategies shown in the table. First,
let's look at the synchronous strategy.
This is the most straightforward
approach in a synchronous setup. The
system waits for every single worker in
this cluster to finish its local
calculation and push its gradient
before performing the global update.
The pro here is a mathematical purity,
because we wait for everyone. We have a
complete picture of the data which
guaranteed convergence. We are
essentially doing true SGD at scale.
However, the con is the straggler
problem we discussed earlier. Your
entire million dollar cluster is held
back by the single slowest worker. If
one node lags, everyone waits. To solve
the weighting problem, we can use the
asynchronous strategy. Here. We don't
wait. As soon as any single worker
finishes its calculation, it pushes its
gradient. The global model updates
immediately, and that worker pulls the
new weight and keeps going. The pro is
incredible speed and high throughput.
Your GPUs are never sitting idle, but
the con is gradient staleness. Because
the workers are moving at different
speeds, a slow worker might submit a
gradient based on a version of the
model that is already 10 steps old.
This can lead to convergence issues and
mathematical instability. Finally,
there is a middle ground approach
called local SGD. Instead of
communicating after each single mini
batch, workers perform many training
steps locally before synchronising with
the rest of the cluster. The pro is
that it drastically reduces
communication cost, which is great for
slow networks. The con, however, is
model drift. Because the workers go so
long without talking to each other,
their local models can start to diverge
in different directions.
Making it harder to reconcile them into
a single accurate global model at the
end.
Choosing between these three isn't
about finding the best one, it's about
choosing the right balance for your
specific hardware and network
constraints. Aggregation is all about
the trade off between speed and
stability. In our next video, we'll
take a deeper look at the convergence
side of this trade-off, specifically
how these different timing strategies
actually change the path your model
takes through the loss landscape. I'll
see you there.
