Many failure modes in deep learning are subtle and not immediately visible. A model may be
overconfident on incorrect predictions. It may generalize poorly to slightly different data
distributions. It may be highly sensitive to small input perturbations that are imperceptible to
humans. It may also perform deceptively well on imbalanced datasets by favoring the dominant classes.
These failures often do not show up when looking at a single aggregate metric. Because of these
complexities, evaluating neural networks requires multiple complementary perspectives. We
need to assess predictive performance using appropriate metrics. We must examine generalization
behavior and overfitting patterns. We should evaluate confidence quality and calibration.
And we need to test robustness and uncertainty. Each of these dimensions reveal a different aspect
of model behavior. And ignoring any of them can lead to misleading conclusions.
