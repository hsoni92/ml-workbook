The gradient tells us how much the loss will change if we nudge the parameters slightly. A positive gradient means increasing the parameter will increase the loss. A negative gradient means increasing the parameter will decrease the loss.
Since the gradient points towards the direction where the loss grows fastest, the optimizer simply moves in the opposite direction. And that's how we reduce the loss. So, when gradient is equal to uphill direction, update is in the downhill direction.
This brings us to the basic gradient descent update rule, which is given by theta nu is equal to theta old minus eta times delta L. Here, theta is the weight that we want to update, eta is the learning rate, and the gradient is the slope at the current point.
The gradient tells us the direction and the learning rate controls how big a step we can take.
Every parameter in the network follows this same rule during the training process.
The learning rate is what makes gradient descent work well or fail. If the learning rate is too small, as we can see in this example, the learning rate is very small, we end up taking very small steps and the training becomes extremely slow.
And if the learning rate is too large, you may overshoot the minimum or even diverge completely with the loss increasing instead of decreasing as can be seen in this example.
A well chosen learning rate creates smooth efficient progress downhill. This is why learning rate tuning is such an important part of model training.
Even though we draw simple curves to explain gradient descent, real neural networks operate in spaces with millions of dimensions. The gradient is a vector containing slopes for every bait and bias in the network.
But the core idea does not change. Take a step in the direction that most reduces the loss. Whether it's one dimension or a million, the logic of gradient descent stays the same.
Now, let us summarize the main points of this video. Gradient descent minimizes loss by stepping downhill. Gradients point towards the direction of steepest increase, so we move opposite to them.
The update rule is simply theta minus learning rate times the gradient. The learning rate determines how fast or how safely we descend. This is the foundation for all optimizers you will see later.
