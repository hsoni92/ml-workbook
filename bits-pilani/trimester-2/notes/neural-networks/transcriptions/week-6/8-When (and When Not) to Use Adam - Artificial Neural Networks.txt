Hello everyone, welcome back to module 6 of Artificial Neural Networks. In this video,
we will focus on a very practical question, when should you use Adam and when should you
avoid it. By the end of this video, you will be able to identify the situations where Adam
is a strong choice and where it may not be ideal. You will be able to understand the trade-off
between fast convergence and generalization. And finally, you will have clear guidelines
for choosing Adam versus other optimizers in real training scenarios. As we have seen
in the previous video, Adam is extremely popular because it works well right out of the box.
It requires very little tuning, it handles noisy gradients well and converges quickly in the
early stages of training. It is also found to be robust across many different architectures.
Because of this, Adam is often the default optimizer and is widely used in NLP models, vision models
and transformers. If you are starting a new project or experimenting with a new architecture,
Adam is usually a safe and reliable first choice. Adam tends to work best in settings where training
is difficult or unstable. This includes deep or complex architectures, models with sparse gradients,
and problems where gradients are noisy, such as large-scale mini-batch training. Adam is also a great choice
during early experimentation when you want fast feedback and want to iterate quickly
and don't want to spend too much time tuning the hyperparameters.
However, Adam is not always the best option. In some cases, especially when final generalization
performance is critical, models trained with Adam may converge to sharp minima that don't generalize
as well. For fine-tuning pre-trained models or in well-conditioned problems, simpler optimizers
like SGD with momentum often outperform Adam in terms of final accuracy. So, while Adam is fast,
it's not always the best choice for the last stage of training. Here's a useful way to think about the
trade-off. Adam converges quickly and is easy to use but may sacrifice some generalization. SGD with
momentum converges more slowly and needs more tuning but often leads to better final performance.
Neither optimizer is universally better. The right choice depends on your goals.
Adam converges quickly and is easy to use. A very common and effective strategy in practice is to combine
both the optimizers. You start training with Adam to quickly reach a good region of the lost landscape.
Once the training stabilizes, you switch to SGD with momentum to fine-tune the model and improve the
generalization. This approach gives you the best of both the worlds. Now, some common mistakes to watch out for.
1. Using Adam blindly without thinking about the task. 2. Never adjusting the learning rates. 3. Using Adam for
final fine-tuning without evaluation. 4. And assuming Adam will always outperform other optimizers.
We need to remember that optimizers are tools, not magic. Choosing them thoughtfully will make a real
difference. 5. Now, let us summarize the main points of this video. Adam is an excellent default optimizer,
especially for complex or noisy problems. 6. It works best for fast convergence and early training.
6. SGD with momentum often performs better for final fine-tuning and generalization.
7. Switching optimizers during training is a common and effective practice.
7. In the next video, we will move on to learning rate scheduling and understand why keeping the
learning rate fixed throughout the training is often limiting. Thank you.
