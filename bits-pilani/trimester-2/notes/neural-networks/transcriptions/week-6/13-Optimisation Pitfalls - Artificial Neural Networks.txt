Often, training starts normally, then slows down, stalls or fluctuates without clear improvement. These issues are frequently caused by optimization challenges rather than bugs in the code or problems with the data.
Let's look at the first pitfall which is the saddle points. A saddle point is a region where the gradient is close to zero but the point is not actually a minimum. See how this looks like the shape of the saddle on horses. Hence the name.
In high-dimensional spaces, saddle points are far more common than local minima. Optimizers can get stuck or move very slowly near saddle points, making it look like training has converged even when it has not. Another common pitfall are the regions of the lost surface that are extremely flat. In these plateaus, gradients are tiny and learning progresses very slowly.
Training may appear to be stuck even though the optimizer is technically still making progress. Plateaus are especially common in deep networks and in models with saturating activation functions. Mini-batch training introduces randomness into gradient updates. This noise can be helpful because it allows the optimizer to escape saddle points. At the same time, it can cause the loss to fluctuate.

Another pitfall is poor conditioning. Poor conditioning occurs when the loss surface has very different curvature in different directions. Some directions are steep while others are nearly flat. This causes zigzag updates, slow progress and high sensitivity to learning rates.
Momentum, adaptive optimizers and normalization techniques help address this problem. At this stage, it is important to distinguish between optimization problems and modeling problems. If the loss does not decrease or behaves erratically, it is usually an optimization issue. If the loss decreases but performance is poor, the issue is more difficult.
If the loss is poor, the issue is more likely related to model capacity or data. This distinction helps you decide what to fix.
Now comes the important question. How do you respond to these optimization pitfalls?
When you encounter optimization issues, possible responses include adjusting the learning rate, switching optimizers, adding momentum or gradient clipping or improving the initialization. These changes target training dynamics, not the model architecture.
