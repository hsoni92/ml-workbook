So, starting with what is a learning rate schedule? A learning rate schedule defines how the learning rate changes as the training progresses. Instead of keeping the learning rate constant, we start with a relatively large value and gradually reduce it over time.
The idea here is simple. Training needs large steps early on and smaller, more precise steps later. Learning rate schedules help match the step size to the current phase of the training.
One of the simplest schedules is step decay. As the name suggests, it follows a step-like pattern. Here, the learning rate stays constant for a while and then drops sharply at predefined points.
For example, we might train with a learning rate of 0.1 for the first 30 epochs, then reduce it to 0.01 and later to 0.001. This works well because early training benefits from large steps while later training benefits from smaller, more stable updates.
However, step decay requires manually choosing when and how much to decay, which can take some tuning.
Next is the exponential decay. Exponential decay reduces the learning rate smoothly over time. Instead of sudden drops, the learning rate is multiplied by a fixed factor at each step or epoch. This creates a continuous gradual reduction.
The advantage is that the decay is smooth and predictable. However, the downside is that if the decay rate is too aggressive, the learning rate can become too small too early, which slows down the training.
Next comes the cosine annealing. Cosine annealing follows a cosine-shaped curve as you can see in the graph. The learning rate starts high, then decreases smoothly and becomes very small near the end.
This smooth transition helps avoid sudden changes in training dynamics. Cosine annealing is very popular, especially for large models and often performs well in practice. It's also commonly used with warm restarts.
Another special scheduling technique is warm-up, which is used at the beginning of training. Instead of starting with a large learning rate, we start with a very small one and gradually increase it over a few steps or epochs.
Warm-up helps prevent unstable updates early in training and is especially important when using large batch sizes or while training the transformers. After the warm-up phase, training switches to another decay schedule such as cosine or step decay. In practice, learning rate schedules are rarely used in isolation. A very common pattern is a short warm-up phase.
A very common pattern is a short warm-up phase followed by a main decay schedule such as cosine annealing or step decay. This combination gives stability at the start, fast progress in the middle and precise fine-tuning near the end.
Now comes the most important practical question which is which schedule should be used.
There is no single best learning rate schedule for all the problems. Step decay is simple and works well for many CNN-based models. Exponential decay is useful when smooth reductions are desired. Cosine annealing is popular in modern deep learning and large-scale training. Warm-up is almost mandatory for transformers and large batch training. There is no fixed answer.
