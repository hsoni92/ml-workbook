makes a prediction, we need a way to measure how good or how bad that prediction is. This is the
role of the loss function. It gives us a number that represents how wrong the model is. During
training, backpropagation uses this number to calculate gradients and update weights.
Different tasks require different loss functions. In this video, we will look at one of the simplest
and most widely used losses, the mean squared error. MSE measures the average squared difference between
the true value and the predicted value. Mathematically, it is 1 by N times sigma y minus y hat squared, where y hat is the predicted y value.
But more important than the formula is the intuition. We take the error, we square it and we average it.
The result is a smooth, always non-negative measure of how far predictions are from the truth. You can also think of MSE geometrically. MSE measures the square distance between the true point and the predicted point. In regression tasks, where predictions lie on a continuous scale, this is exactly what we want. We are essentially asking the network to position its predictions as close as possible to the true value.
Let's make this concrete with a small example.
Even in this tiny example, you can see how MSE punishes large errors much more strongly than the small ones.
MSE is the default loss function for regression. It's used when the output is a continuous value.
Predicting house prices, it works well because it directly measures distance in numerical space. But for classification, MSE behaves poorly and we will see why in the next video.
