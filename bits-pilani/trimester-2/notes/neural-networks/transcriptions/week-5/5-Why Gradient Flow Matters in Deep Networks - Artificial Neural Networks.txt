you will understand what gradient flow means in deep neural networks, how gradients propagate through
many layers, why extremely small and extremely large gradients are both harmful, and why increasing
depth makes training fundamentally harder. In the previous lesson, we saw that backpropagation
computes gradients of the error with respect to every parameter in the network. These gradients
do not just appear at one place. They travel backward through the entire network from the
output layer all the way to the first layer. This backward movement of gradients is what we call
gradient flow. A very important idea to keep in mind is this. A layer can only learn if a usable
gradient actually reaches it. If gradients do not reach a layer properly, that layer simply cannot
improve. When gradient flow is good, gradients reach all layers with a meaningful size and a stable direction. This allows all the layers to learn together. When a gradient flow is poor, two things can happen. Gradients can become extremely small or gradients can become extremely large. In both the cases, learning breaks down. So, gradient flow is good.
So, is not just a mathematical concept. It is literally a health indicator for training.
Why does the gradient flow is good. In a deep network, gradients travel through many layers. At every layer, the gradient is multiplied by a local derivative. So, by the time the gradient reaches the early layers, it is the product of many numbers. If most of these numbers are less than 1, then gradients shrink rapidly. If they are greater than 1, gradient flow will be greater than 1.
Gradients grow rapidly. This repeated multiplication causes gradients. This repeated multiplication causes gradients to vanish or explode exponentially as the depth increases. This is not a software problem or a hardware problem. It is a fundamental mathematical consequence of depth. There are exactly two fundamental ways gradient flow can fail. The first is vanishing gradients. Gradients become extremely close to zero. Early layers
receive almost no learning signal. Training becomes painfully slow or completely stuck. The second is exploding gradients. Gradients grow extremely large. Parameter updates become unstable. Training diverges numerically. Both of these are direct symptoms of overgradient flow. The earliest layers in a deep network are farthest from the error. This means they receive
gradients after many gradients after many multiplications. So, even if the output layers are learning well, the early layers may receive almost no signal in vanishing gradient cases or they receive chaotic signals in exploding gradient cases. This leads to a very common failure mode. Deep networks behave like shallow networks because early layers, they never truly learn. During real training, gradient flow can be treated as a diagnostic
signal. In healthy training. In healthy training. Gradients across layers remain reasonably scaled. No layer is completely starved of learning signal. In unhealthy training, gradients collapse to zero in early layers or gradients blow up unpredictably. Modern deep learning practitioners often monitor gradient norms across layers to detect these failures early. So, now comes the question that what determines
whether gradient flow will be whether gradient flow will be healthy or unstable. Several factors play a dominant role. The activation function, how the weights are initialized, the depth of the network, and even the numerical precision of computation. Gradient flow. Gradient flow describes how learning signals propagate through depth. In deep networks, gradients undergo repeated multiplication.
