Hello everyone. Welcome to module 8 of Artificial Neural Networks. In this module, we will study a new class of models designed specifically for spatial data, convolutional neural networks, that power the modern computer vision system.
So far in this course, we have focused primarily on fully connected neural networks. We studied how multilayer perceptrons learn representations, how information flows forward through layers, and how these models are trained using backpropagation and optimization techniques. A key assumption in all of this was that the input is treated as a flat vector.
Each input dimension is handled independently without any notion of structure or relationships between neighboring inputs. This assumption works well for many types of data, but it also has important limitations.
The model does not explicitly capture relationships between nearby inputs. Second, the number of parameters grow very quickly as the input size increases. For structured data, such as images, this leads to inefficient models that require many parameters and large amounts of data to train effectively.
This motivates the need for architectures that can exploit structure in the input. Images are fundamentally different from generic vectors.
Pixels that are highly correlated and meaningful patterns such as edges or corners are formed by local groups of pixels. At the same time, the same kind of patterns can appear at many different locations within an image.

spatial locations, a concept known as weight sharing. As a result, the model learns what patterns to
detect, independent of where those patterns appear in the image. This makes TNN far more
efficient and better suited for spatial data. Another property of CNNs is hierarchical feature
learning. In early layers, CNNs typically learn very simple patterns such as edges and corners.
As we move deeper into the network, these simple patterns are combined to form more complex features
like textures, shapes, shapes, and eventually entire objects. This hierarchical structure allows CNNs to transform
raw pixel values into increasingly abstract and meaningful representations. In this module, we will build a

Finally, we will look at classic and modern CNN models to understand how architectural design has evolved over time. By the end of this module, you will understand both the mechanics and the conceptual foundations of convolutional neural networks.
