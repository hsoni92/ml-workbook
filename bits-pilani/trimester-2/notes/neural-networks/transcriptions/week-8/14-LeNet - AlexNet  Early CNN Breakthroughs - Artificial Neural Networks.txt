AlexNet marked a major turning point in the history of convolutional neural networks.
If you look at the architecture diagram, you will notice that it follows the same high-level structure as LeNet, but at a much larger scale.
AlexNet uses many more convolutional layers and significantly more filters.
The depth of the network is much greater, allowing it to learn richer and more abstract features.
Importantly, the architecture clearly separates convolutional feature extraction from fully connected classification layers.
AlexNet showed that CNNs could scale to large real-world image datasets and outperform traditional computer vision approaches by a large margin.
AlexNet was not just larger, it introduced several ideas that made deep CNNs practical.
It relied heavily on ReLU activations, which helped address training difficulties in deep networks.
It leveraged GPUs to make large-scale training computationally feasible.
Regularization techniques such as dropout were also used to reduce overfitting.
Together, these design choices demonstrated that deep convolutional networks could be trained effectively when data, hardware, and architecture design are aligned.
