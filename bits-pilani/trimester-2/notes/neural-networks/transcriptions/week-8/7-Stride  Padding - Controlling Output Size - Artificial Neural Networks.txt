Hello everyone, welcome back to module 8 of Artificial Neural Networks. In this video, we will study two important design choices in convolutional layers, stride and padding.
By the end of this video, you will be able to understand how stride affects the movement of the filter, why padding is introduced and how these choices control the spatial size of feature maps. Each convolution operation changes the spatial dimension of its input. If we apply multiple convolutions without careful design, feature maps can shrink very quickly.
The network may lose important information. This makes output size an important design consideration rather than a side effect of convolution.
Stride determines how far the filter moves across the input at each step. With a stride of 1, the filter moves 1 pixel at a time. With a larger stride, the filter skips over positions.
This directly affects how densely the filter samples the input. Stride therefore controls the sampling resolution of the convolution operation.
When stride increases, the filter is applied at fewer spatial locations. As a result, the output feature map becomes smaller.
This reduces computational cost but also reduces spatial detail. Choosing stride involves a trade-off between efficiency and the level of spatial precision retained in the feature maps.

Next comes the concept of padding. In padding, we apply extra pixels around the input. Without padding, convolution near the edges of an image becomes problematic.
Border pixels are used fewer times than central pixels and information near the edges is lost quickly.
Padding addresses this issue by adding extra pixels around the input. This allows filters to be applied more uniformly across the image.
Padding also gives us control over the output dimensions.
By adding padding, we can preserve spatial dimensions or slow down the reduction in size across layers.
This makes padding a useful tool for maintaining spatial information while building deeper networks.
Common choices include no padding and same padding.
