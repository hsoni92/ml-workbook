By the end of this video, you will be able to understand how stacking convolutional layers builds hierarchical representations and how increasing depth allows CNNs to capture increasingly complex patterns in the data.
Let's begin by considering what a single convolutional layer can achieve. A single convolutional layer is effective at detecting simple local patterns such as edges or basic textures.
However, real-world images contain much more complex structures. Capturing such complexity requires combining multiple simple patterns which cannot be achieved with just one convolutional layer.
This limitation motivates the need for deeper network. When we stack convolutional layers, the output of one layer becomes the input to the next. Instead of operating directly on raw pixels, deeper layers operate on feature maps produced by earlier layers. As a result, each layer has access to a larger effective context of the input image. This increasing context is often described in terms of an increasing response.
The input of an increasing receptive field. Stacking layers leads naturally to hierarchical feature learning. Early layers learn basic visual primitives such as edges and corners. Middle layers combine these primitives into textures and repeated motifs. Deeper layers combine these patterns into more meaningful structures such as parts of the object. This hierarchy allows CNNs to represent complex concepts efficiently.
