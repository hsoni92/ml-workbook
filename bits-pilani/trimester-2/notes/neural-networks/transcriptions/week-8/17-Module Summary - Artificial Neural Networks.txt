In this module, we studied convolutional neural networks, one of the most important architectures.
We focused on understanding why CNNs are well suited for image and spatial data, and how their design leverages local structure, parameter sharing, and hierarchical feature learning.
We began by introducing the core ideas behind CNNs. We studied the convolution operation and saw how it produces feature maps that capture local spatial patterns.
We discussed filters as learnable pattern detectors and how multiple channels and multiple filters allow CNNs to build rich representations from raw images.
These concepts form the foundation of how CNNs process spatially structured data.
Next, we examined how CNNs control the evolution of representations across layers. Stride and padding determine how spatial resolution changes during convolution.
Pooling reduces spatial dimensions and introduces robustness to small spatial shifts. As representations move deeper into the network, spatial detail gradually decreases while semantic richness increases.
This spatial to semantic transformation is a defining characteristic of CNNs. We then brought these ideas together into a complete CNN pipeline. Convolutional blocks extract spatial features, stacking layers builds hierarchical representations, and flattening converts feature maps into vector representations. Fully connected layers then aggregate global information and produce final predictions.

Early models like LeNet demonstrated that convolutional networks could work in practice. AlexNet showed that deep CNNs could scale to large datasets and achieve dramatic performance improvements.
VGG highlighted the power of depth through simple, repeatable design, while ResNet introduced residual connections that made very deep networks trainable. Finally, we discussed how modern CNN design is shaped by real-world constraints.
