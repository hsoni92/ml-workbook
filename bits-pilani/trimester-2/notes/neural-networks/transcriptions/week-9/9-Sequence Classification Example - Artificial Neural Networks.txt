as a sequence of words, one word at a time. Each word represents a time step in the sequence and the model processes the sentence from left to right.

After the entire sequence has been processed, the model uses the final hidden state to make a prediction.
This hidden state is assumed to encode the relevant information from the entire sequence.
It is passed through a simple classification layer to produce the final output, such as positive or negative sentiment.
This pattern, where a sequence is mapped to a single output using the final hidden state, is typical for sequence classification tasks.
For longer or more complex sequences, basic RNNs may struggle to retain important early information.
This is where LSTMs and GRUs become especially useful.
Their gating mechanisms help the model decide what information to keep, what to forget, and what to use for prediction.
In our example, these gates helps to ensure that the word not is remembered until it is needed to interpret good correctly.
As a result, gated models perform much better on tasks involving long-range dependencies.
Now, let us summarize the main points of this video.
Sequence classification involves mapping an entire sequence to a single output.
Sequence models process inputs one step at a time and hidden states act as a memory that accumulates information over time.
The final hidden state is used to make a sequence-level prediction.
LSTM and GRU architectures improve this process by enabling better retention of important contexts across long sequences.
