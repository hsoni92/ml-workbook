Hello everyone. Welcome to Module 7 of Artificial Neural Networks, where we'll focus on regularization and generalization, learning how to prevent over-fitting and build models that perform well on unseen data.
You now understand how neural networks learn. In the previous modules, we studied how neural networks are trained. We saw how gradients are computed using backpropagation and how optimization algorithms use those gradients to update the model parameters.
At this point, a natural question arises. If a model trains well, if the loss decreases and accuracy improves, does that mean the model is actually good?
As we will see in this module, the answer is not always.
What does it really mean for a neural network to work? Is it enough to have high training accuracy, low training loss, or is the real goal something else?
In practice, neural networks are not evaluated on the data they were trained on. They are evaluated on new unseen data. A model that performs well only on training data but fails on new inputs is not useful, no matter how impressive its training metrics look.
This brings us to a key concept that is generalization. Modern neural networks are extremely powerful. They can fit complex patterns, memorize large datasets, even learn noise in the data. Because of this, achieving good training performance is often easy.
But good training performance can be misleading. A model may appear to learn well while actually memorizing the training data instead of learning meaningful patterns. The gap between training performance and real-world performance is known as the generalization problem. And it is one of the central challenges in deep learning.
In this module, we will study how to address this challenge. We will begin by understanding what generalization means, how underfitting and overfitting occur, the bias-variance trade-off. We will then explore practical techniques used to improve generalization, including regularization methods and some practical strategies like dropout and batch normalization, early stopping, data augmentation,
training setup choices. The goal is not just to train neural networks, but to train reliable ones. So, let's try to answer the key question, why generalization matters? In real-world applications, models are deployed on future data, data that they have never seen before. Poor generalization leads to unstable predictions, poor real-world performance, models that look good in experience,
experiments, but they fail in production. Ultimately, generalization determines whether a neural network is actually useful or not. In the next video, we will formally define what generalization means and understand why it is the true objective of neural network training. Thank you.
