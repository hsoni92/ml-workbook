When a model makes errors on unseen data, those errors do not all come from the same source.
Some errors arise because the model makes overly simple assumptions about the data.
Other errors arise because the model is overly sensitive to the specific training examples it sees.
The concept of bias and variance capture these two different sources of generalization error.
Bias refers to the error introduced by simplifying assumptions made by a model.
A high bias model is too rigid and unable to represent the true complexity of the data.
As a result, it makes systematic errors regardless of how much training data it sees.
High bias is typically associated with underfitting, where the model fails to capture important patterns in the data.
Variance refers to how sensitive a model is to the particular training data it is given.
A high variance model can change significantly when trained on slightly different data sets.
Such models tend to fit noise along with meaningful signals, leading to unstable predictions on unseen data.
High variance is commonly associated with overfitting.
Bias and variance are not independent of each other.
As you can see in this curve, as the model complexity increases, the bias reduces while the variance increases.
On the other hand, when the model complexity decreases, the bias increases and the variance reduces.
So there is a trade-off between the two.
Improving one often versens the another one.
We can now connect these ideas to the behaviors we have studied earlier.
Underfitting occurs when bias is high and variance is low.
Overfitting occurs when bias is low and variance is high.
Good generalization lies in between these two extremes, where the model is flexible enough to capture patterns, but stable enough to ignore the noise.
Now, let us summarize the main points of this video.
Bias and variance provide a useful framework for understanding generalization errors.
The goal in model design and training is to balance the two.
This framework guides decisions about model complexity, data usage and training strategies.
