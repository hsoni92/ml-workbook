By the end of this video, you will be able to understand why multiple regularization techniques
are often used together, identify common and effective combinations, and you will be aware
of potential pitfalls when combining them. No single regularization method addresses all sources
of overfitting. Different techniques act at different stages of the learning process. Some
constrain the model parameters, others inject noise, and others limit training duration.
Combining regularization methods allows us to control overfitting from multiple angles,
making the model more robust. Each regularization technique targets a different failure mode.
L2 regularization limits the magnitude of weights and encourages smoother solutions. Dropout prevents
neurons from co-adapting too strongly. Batch normalization stabilizes activations and introduces noise through batch statistics.
Early stopping prevents overtraining and data augmentation increases the diversity of the training data.
Together, these methods address complementary aspects of generalization. In practice, certain combinations are widely used.
L2 regularization is commonly paired with data augmentation to control model complexity while increasing data diversity. Batch normalization is often used together with moderate dropout. Early stopping is typically combined with learning rate scheduling to control the training duration.
Smaller batch sizes are often used alongside L2 regularization. Smaller batch sizes are also frequently used alongside L2 regularization to improve the generalization. When combining regularization methods, it is important to be aware of the interactions. For example, batch normalization already introduces noise which can reduce the need for strong dropout. Very strong dropout combined with very small batch sizes can make the training.
Unstable. Adding too many regularization. Adding too many regularization techniques at once can also lead to underfitting. The key is to balance regularization strength rather than maximizing it. Adjust the strength of each method gradually.
