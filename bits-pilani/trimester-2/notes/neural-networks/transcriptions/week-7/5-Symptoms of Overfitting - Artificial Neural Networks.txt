Overfitting does not usually happen suddenly. In many cases, it develops gradually as the training
progresses. Because training metrics often continue to improve, it can be difficult to notice overfitting
if we focus only on the training performance. Detecting overfitting early helps us to
avoid wasted computation and prevents us from selecting models that perform poorly in real-world
settings. One of the clearest symptoms of overfitting appears when we compare training and validation
curves. As we can see in this graph, as the model keeps on training, the training loss appears to
decrease, while the validation loss stops improving or maybe after a point it starts increasing
also. The growing gap between training and validation performance is a strong signal that the model is no longer learning
patterns that generalize. Another common symptom of overfitting is instability in the validation metrics. Validation accuracy may fluctuate significantly from epoch to epoch. Small changes in the validation data or the random seed can lead to noticeably different results. This instability indicates that the model is overly sensitive to specific training examples it has seen.
Overfitting can also be observed in the behavior of the model itself. Overfitting can also be observed in the behavior of the model itself. Such models often make highly confident predictions on training-like inputs but perform poorly on slightly different or shifted inputs. They may fail to generalize even to simple variations that a robust model should handle. This reflects memorization rather than meaningful learning.
