training and validation performance. Look for the signs such as a large gap between training and
validation metrics, validation loss increasing over time, or highly unstable validation performance.
Before changing anything, ask a simple but critical question. Is the model underfitting or is it overfitting?
Without this diagnosis, it is easy to apply the wrong fix. Many generalization issues originate from the data rather than the model. Check whether the data set is sufficiently large and diverse. Ensure that the validation set is representative of real-world data and that there is no data leakage between training and validation. Next, revisit the model complexity. Ask whether the model is too large for the amount of data
available. If necessary, reduce the depth or width or introduce regularization such as L2
penalties or dropout. The goal is not to use the most complex model possible,
but the simplest model that performs well. Excessive capacity without sufficient data
is a common cause of overfitting. Training configuration also plays a major role in
generalization. Check whether the batch size is too large, whether the learning rate is too small,
or whether training is running for too many epochs. Ensure that early stopping is enabled and that
validation metrics are being monitored. Small adjustments to training dynamics can significantly
improve the generalization. Now, let us summarize the main points of this video.

intervention. Effective solutions often involve a combination of data level, model level,
