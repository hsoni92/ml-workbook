Hello everyone. Welcome back to Module 7 of Artificial Neural Networks. In this video,
we will focus on how training configuration choices influence generalization. By the end
of this video, you will be able to identify training configuration choices that influence
generalization. You will be able to understand how batch size, learning rate, model capacity,
and validation strategy affect overfitting and generalization, and how to make practical
training setup decisions. Generalization is not controlled only by explicit regularization
techniques such as L1, L2, or dropout. How a model is trained also plays a major role.
Training setup choices shape learning dynamics and can implicitly regularize the model. Even
with strong regularization, a poor training setup can still lead to overfitting. Let us start
with the batch size. Batch size has a strong influence on generalization. Smaller batch sizes
introduce noise into gradient estimates. This noise can help the optimizer avoid sharp minima
that fit the training data too precisely. Larger batch sizes produce smoother and more stable
updates, but they may converge to solutions that generalize less. In practice, smaller batches
often lead to better generalization. Next is the learning rate. The learning rate controls how quickly
model parameters change. Very large learning rates can make training unstable.
Very small learning rates allow the model to fit the training data extremely precisely, increasing the risk of memorization.
And finally, the model capacity. The learning rate must also be considered together with the number of training epochs
since both determine how closely the model fits the data. And finally, the model capacity.
Model capacity refers to how expressive the network is. Models with insufficient capacity
underfit, while models with excessive capacity overfit. The appropriate capacity depends on both the size
and complexity of the dataset. A practical strategy is to start with a simpler model and increase complexity
gradually as needed. Good training setup always includes monitoring generalization. This requires a representative validation set. During training, validation metrics should be tracked alongside training loss. Early stopping can then be used to halt training once validation performance stops improving. Without proper monitoring, it is difficult to find out.
It is difficult to diagnose or prevent over-footing. Now, let us summarize the main points of this video.
Training setup choices strongly influence generalization.
