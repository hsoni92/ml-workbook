which is the learning rate. By the end of this video, you will be able to explain what the
learning rate actually does inside gradient descent, how it affects the stability of training,
and how to recognize the symptoms of learning rates that are too high or too low. The learning
rate controls how far the parameters move after each gradient update. Think of gradient descent
as walking downhill on a complicated landscape. The gradient tells you which direction to walk,
but the learning rate decides how big a step you can take. If the step is too small, you may make
very slow progress. If it is too large, you can overshoot and bounce around instead of moving
downhill. We already know this formula of learning rate. Mathematically, the learning rate multiplies
the gradient in every update. That means it scales every change to every weight in the network. So, even if the gradients are computed perfectly, a badly chosen learning rate will distort how those gradients are applied. When the learning rate is too small, the model does learn but extremely slowly. The loss curve goes down but at a very gentle slope. This makes training inefficient and can also cause the model to get stuck in some way.
Suboptimal solutions, especially in deep networks. Now, when the learning rate is too large, the
opposite happens. Instead of the opposite happens. Instead of smoothly moving towards a minimum, the parameters keep overshooting it. This causes the loss to oscillate, sometimes violently, and in extreme cases, the training can completely diverge. Learning rate also interacts with other hyperparameters. Small back sizes, large models, and regularization all make training more sensitive to learning rate.
So, learning rate acts like a central knob that connects all parts of the training process. Rather than just talking about learning rate, we will now look at it in practice.
