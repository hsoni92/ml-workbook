If we run the same code again under the same conditions, we should obtain the same results.
In traditional software systems, this is usually taken for granted. However, in deep learning,
achieving reproducibility is surprisingly difficult because training neural networks involves many
stochastic components that are often hidden from view. Let's look at why reproducibility breaks in
practice. Neural networks typically start with randomly initialized weights. Training data is often
different than shuffled differently in each run. Many GPU operations are non-deterministic for performance reasons.
Techniques like dropout deliberately introduce randomness during training. And finally, multi-threading and parallel execution can change the order of computations. Each of these factors can slightly alter training dynamics leading to different final models. This lack of reproducibility is not just an academic concern, but it's not just an academic concern.
It is genuinely dangerous. If results are not reproducible, you cannot fully trust model improvements. Debugging becomes extremely difficult because you cannot isolate the cause of a change. Most importantly, you cannot clearly compare experiments, which undermines both research conclusions and production decision-making. To achieve reproducibility, several elements must be explicitly controlled. We need to fix random seeds across libraries.
We must ensure consistent data splits for training, validation and testing. All hyperparameters should be logged and reused exactly. Finally, software versions including frameworks, drivers and hardware dependencies must be tracked because even small changes can affect the outcome.
