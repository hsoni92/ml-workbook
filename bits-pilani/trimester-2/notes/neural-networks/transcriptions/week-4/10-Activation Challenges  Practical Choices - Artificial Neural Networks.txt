Activation functions are not just a mathematical formality. They strongly influence how a neural network trains, converges, and remains stable.
A poor activation choice can lead to extremely slow learning, unstable training, or in some cases, no learning at all.
That is why activation selection is not a minor design detail. It is a core architectural decision that directly impacts whether a deep network will work in practice or not.
Let us start with the problem of saturation, which occurs primarily with sigmoid and tanage. For very large positive or very large negative inputs, both sigmoid and tanage flatten out. In these regions, even a large change in the input produces only a tiny change in the output.
During training, this means that learning signals become extremely weak. As a result, parameter updates become very small and learning slows down dramatically.
This was one of the main reasons why early deep neural networks were so difficult to train. Saturation leads directly to another major challenge known as the vanishing gradient problem.
When signals are passed backward through many layers, small values multiply with each other and become even smaller. As a result, the early layers in a deep network receive almost no learning signal.
This means that the deeper the network, the harder it becomes for the first layers to learn meaningful features. We will study the mathematical details of this phenomena later. For now, it is important to understand its conceptual impact on training deep networks.
Although, ReLU solved the saturation problem in the positive region, it introduced a new issue known as the dying ReLU problem. Recall that, ReLU solved the saturation problem in the positive region. It introduced a new issue known as the dying ReLU problem.
Recall that, ReLU outputs exactly zero for all negative inputs. If, during training, a neuron starts receiving inputs that are always negative, it will output zero for every input thereafter. Once this happens, the neuron effectively stops contributing to the network. It no longer activates and it no longer learns. Such neurons are referred to as dead ReLUs.
This problem does not always occur, but when it does, it reduces the effective capacity of the network. Another less common but dangerous issue is exploding activations. In some deep networks, activations can grow very large as they propagate through layers. This can happen due to poor weight initialization, excessive depth or unstable activation weight interactions.
