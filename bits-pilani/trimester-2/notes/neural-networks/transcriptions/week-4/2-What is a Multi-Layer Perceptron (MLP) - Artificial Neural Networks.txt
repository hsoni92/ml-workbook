So, let's start with what is a multi-layer perceptron. A multi-layer perceptron or MLP is one of the most fundamental architectures in neural networks.
Formally, an MLP is a feed-forward neural network. It is composed of three types of layers: an input layer, one or more hidden layers and an output layer. Information flows strictly in one direction, from the input layer to the hidden layers and then to the output layer. There are no cycles and no feedback connections in an MLP.
This one-way flow of information is what we mean by feed-forward. Let us now look more closely at what each of these layers does. The input layer simply holds the raw feature vector. No computation happens here. It only serves as a container for the data. The hidden layers are where the actual transformations occur. Each hidden layer takes the output of the previous layer and
produces a new transformed representation. Finally, the output layer takes the last hidden representation and produces the final prediction of the network. In most standard MLPs, neurons in one layer are fully connected to neurons in the next layer. Meaning, every neuron in one layer sends its output to every neuron in the next layer.
