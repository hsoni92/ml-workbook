So far, we have seen that sigmoid is suitable for binary classification, where there are only two possible outcomes. However, in many real-world problems, we do not have just two classes. For example, recognizing handwritten digits involves 10 classes, classifying news articles may involve many topics, and image recognition can involve hundreds or thousands of categories.
In such cases, the model must output one probability per class, and all these probabilities must be non-negative and they must sum up to 1. This is exactly the requirement that the softmax function is designed to satisfy.
Before softmax is applied, the final layer of the network produces a vector of real-valued numbers called logits. These logits are simply the raw outputs of the last linear layer. They can be positive or negative, and they can be small or large, and do not have any probabilistic meaning yet. At this stage, the network has only produced unnormalized scores for each of the class.
We still need a transformation that will convert these scores into valid probabilities. That transformation is softmax. The softmax function takes a vector of logits and converts it into a probability distribution. For a vector, let's say z equals z1, z2, zk. The softmax output for the ith class is defined as:
zi equals e to the power zi divided by summation of e to the power zj for j ranging from 1 to k. There are three key properties of this transformation. Every output lies between 0 and 1. All outputs sum up to 1. And larger logits get larger probabilities. So, softmax converts arbitrary real numbers into a valid categorical number.
