Now, let's talk about one of the most important and widely used security concepts in
AWS. IAM rules and trust relationships. To understand IAM rules, let's first clear one
thing up.
An IAM rule is not a user. It doesn't have a username, it doesn't have a password, and
it doesn't have anything related to permanent access keys. Instead, an IAM rule is a
temporary identity that can be assumed when needed.
In simple terms, a rule allows something, like an application or an AWS service, to
temporarily act with certain permissions.
So, why did AWS introduce rules in the first place? Earlier, applications stored AWS
access keys directly inside the servers or the code. This caused serious security
problems where the keys got deleted or reused incorrectly.
So, IAM rules solve this by producing temporary credentials that are automatically
managed by AWS. We don't need to worry about it. So, no secrets are stored and
credentials expire automatically and get renewed automatically.
This is why rules are always the preferred way to grant access in AWS. Now, let's look
at where rules are used in real life. If an EC2 compute service needs to access S3
bucket for storage, we don't give it an IAM user.
Instead, the EC2 instance assumes a role that has permissions to access this S3
bucket. Similarly, there are multiple services which we could have a communication
between them through these IAM rules. Now comes a very important concept, which
is trust relationships.
Permissions alone are not enough. Every IAM role has two sides. One side defines
what actions are allowed, like reading from a storage or writing to a database.
The other side defines who is allowed to assume the role. And this is called a trust
relationship. You can think of it like this.
A trust policy answers the question like who is allowed to step into this role. Without a
trust relationship, the role cannot be used, even if it has permissions. A simple way to
remember this is trust policies decides who can enter and the permission poli cy
decides what they can do once they are inside.
So, rules can trust AWS services like EC2, Lambda and other AWS accounts or even
external identity systems.
