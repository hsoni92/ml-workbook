Managed vs self-managed databases transcript

In this topic, we are going to understand a very important  design decision. Choosing
between managed databases and self -managed databases running on EC2. This
choice affects how much effort your team spends on operations versus building
futures.

Let's start by clarifying what these two actually mean. A self -managed database
means you launch an EC2 instance and instal the database all by yourself. You decide
how it is configured, how it is secured, and how backups work and scaling happens
on its own.

In contrast, a managed database means AWS takes care of most of that operational
responsibility on your behalf. You focus on your data and application logic while AWS
handles maintenance tasks in the background. Now, with self -managed databases,
you are re sponsible for everything, whether it is OS updates, database patches,
backups, monitoring, replication, and even failover.

This gives maximum flexibility but also maximum responsibility. Teams usually choose
this model when they need very specific configurations or unsupported database
versions because there are certain plugins that managed services don't allow. On the
other hand, managed databases remove a lot of operational burden.
Backups happen automatically. Failover is built in. Monitoring works out of the box.

So, scaling storage or adding replicas becomes much more easier. A helpful way to
think about this is ownership versus service. Running a self-managed database is like
owning and renting your own data centre equipment.

You control everything, but you also fix everything. Using a managed database is like
using a professionally operated facility where power, cooling, maintenance,
monitoring, all will be taken care of. From a cloud -native perspective, most modern
architectures prefer managed databases.

They reduce risk, save time, and allow teams to focus on delivering futures instead of
managing infrastructure. That said, self -managed databases still have their place,
especially in legacy systems or compliance -heavy environments or when deep
customisation is required. So, the key takeaway here is simple.

Start with managed databases by default. Only choose self-managed when you have
a clear, justified reason to do so. In the next topic, we'll look at the actual AWS
database services available, including RDS, DynamoDB, and Aurora. We'll also try to
understand how they fit into different application needs.
