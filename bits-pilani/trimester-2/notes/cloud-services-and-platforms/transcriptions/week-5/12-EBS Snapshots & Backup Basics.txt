EBS Snapshots & Backup Basics Transcript
In this topic, we are going to understand EBS snapshots, how backup works in AWS,
how data is restored when something goes wrong. Let's start simple. An EBS snapshot
is a point in time backup of an EBS volume.
It captures the data exactly as it exists at that moment. Think of it like taking a photo
of your disc. Whatever data is there at that time gets saved safely by AWS.
Now, why do we even need snapshots? Because systems fail, people make mistakes,
volumes can get deleted, updates will also go wrong in some places. So, snapshots
act as your safety net. If something breaks, you don't panic, you just restore.
So, snapshots are stored by AWS and are incremental. That means the first snapshot
is a full backup and every snapshot after that only saves what has changed. This
makes them fast and cost effective.
Now, let's talk about backup strategy in a very particular way. So, a good backup
strategy answers three questions. What do I backup? How often do I back it up? And
how long do I keep it? Critical data might need daily snapshots.
Less important systems might be weekly. In real environments, backups are almost
always automated. Teams don't rely on someone remembering to click create
snapshot.
Now, an important concept, restoring from a snapshot. You never restore a snapshot
directly into a running EC2 instance. Instead, the flow is always snapshot, then it will
create a new EBS volume, we attach it to EC2, then access the data.
For root disc, you can even launch a brand new EC2 instance from an existing
snapshot. A simple way to think about snapshot is like phone backups. If your phone
crashes, you don't fix the broken system, you restore the last working backup.
Same idea here as well. The key takeaway is this, snapshots are simple, powerful,
and mandatory in real AWS environments. No production system runs safely without
a proper backup and a restore plan.
