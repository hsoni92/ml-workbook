What is EC2? When people talk about AWS, one of the first service you'll hear about
is EC2. EC2 stands for Elastic Compute Cloud and simply put, it allows you to run
virtual servers in the AWS cloud. Think of an EC2 instance just like a real server.

It has its own CPU, memory, storage, and networking. You can instal an operating
system, deploy applications, and connect to it just like you would with a physical
machine. The big difference is you don't buy the hardware.

AWS owns and manages these physical servers. You just rent the compute power
when you need it. Now, why is it called elastic? Elastic means flexible.

With EC2, you can launch one server today and add 10 more tomorrow if the traffic
increases. The flexibility is what makes EC2 so powerful. In traditional setups,
companies had to buy servers up front.

They had to guess the future capacity, spend a lot of money, and still risk running out
of resources during this peak usage. With EC2, that problem completely disappears.
You launch server in minutes, scale up or scale down anytime, and pay only for the
time the servers are running.

This is the reason why EC2 is used for many things like testing your applications,
processing your data, and even running temporary scripts or jobs. A simple way to
think about EC2 is it's like renting an apartment instead of buying a house. You choose
the size you need, move in quickly, pay only while you stay, and move out whenever
you want.

So the building maintenance is not your responsibility. And that is why EC2 is so
important to understand. It is the foundation of compute in AWS, and almost every
world AWS architecture uses EC2 in some form.

So AWS starts to make a lot more sense because we'll go deeper and look at the key
components of EC2 like AMI, instance, types, and key pairs.
