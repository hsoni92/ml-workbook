What is CloudTrail Transcript

In this topic, we are going to understand AWS CloudTrail, which plays an important
role in security, auditing, and compliance. CloudTrail answers one simple but powerful
question. Who did what and when inside your AWS account? Whenever someone
performs an action directly through the console or through some commands or even
another AWS service, CloudTrail records it.

Think of CloudTrail as an activity log for your entire AWS environment. CloudTrail
records actions such as creating or deleting resources, modifying security settings,
starting or stopping instances, changing any IAM policies, or accessing sensitive
services. Each recorded event contains details like who performed the action, what
action was performed, which service was involved, and the time it happened.

This makes CloudTrail extremely valuable during investigations or audits. CloudTrail
events are categorised into three types. The first is management events.

These track control plane actions like creating or modifying the resources. They are
enabled by default and form the backbone of auditing. The second type is data events.

These track high-volume operations such as reading or writing S3 objects or invoking
your Lambda functions. Because they generate a lot of logs, they are optional and
enabled only when needed. The third type is inside events, which automatically
detects unusual activity patterns, for example, sudden spikes in your API calls.

Once events are captured, CloudTrail delivers them to the storage. Logs are typically
sent to an S3 bucket for long -term retention. They can also be sent to CloudWatch
logs if you want near real-time monitoring or alerting.

This allows security teams to search and analyse what is going on. To understand
CloudTrail, think of it like a CCTV footage or an access log inside an office building.
Every bad swipe, door access, or system change gets recorded.

Even if something goes wrong later on, you can rewind and see exactly what
happened. That is why CloudTrail is critical for security audits, incident response,
regulatory compliance, and accountability. One important distinction to remember
here, CloudTrail does not tell you how busy your system is.

It tells you who made changes and what actions were taken. So do not get it confused
with the CloudWatch logs and metrics. Performance monitoring belongs to
CloudWatch.
