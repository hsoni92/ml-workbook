Now, let's look at how CloudTrail actually becomes useful in the real environments by
integrating with S3 and CloudWatch. By itself, CloudTrail only records events. To store
those events safely and to act on them, we connect it to other AWS services.
The first and most important integration is with Amazon S3. When CloudTrail is
enabled, it continuously delivers log files into an S3 bucket. These logs are stored as
structured files and organised by account, region, and time.
S3 is used because it's durable, cost -effective, and it is ideal for long -term storage.
Organisations often keep these logs for months or years to meet the compliance and
audit requirements. Think of S3 as the archive room where all activity history is safely
preserved.
This is the place where we use the storage classes concept with a combination of
lifecycle rules. CloudWatch allows CloudTrail events to be streamed into the log
groups where they can be searched, filtered, and monitored almost immediately. This
makes it possible to detect important activities as they happen, not hours later on.
You can detect when someone logs in as a root, when IAM permissions are changed,
or when a critical resource is deleted. Once events reach CloudWatch, you can create
alarms. These alarms can send notifications, trigger some automation, or even take a
corrective action.
In many environments, CloudWatch alarms are connected to email alerts, Slack
messages, or Lambda functions that get triggered automatically. Together, these
create a powerful flow. How the flow looks like is an action happens, CloudTrail records
it, S3 will store it, CloudWatch will monitor it, alerts and automations will take place.
This integration is foundational for security, auditing, and governance in AWS. Almost
every production AWS environment relies on this particular setup. Understanding how
CloudTrail works with S3 and CloudWatch helps you see how AWS builds visibility,
accountability, and trust into the platform.
