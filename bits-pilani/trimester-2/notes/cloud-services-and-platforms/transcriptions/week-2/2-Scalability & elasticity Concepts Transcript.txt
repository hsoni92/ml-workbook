Scalability & elasticity Concepts Transcript
In this video, we are going to explore two foundational cloud concepts, which is
scalability and elasticity. These concepts determine whether a system can grow, adapt
to a changing demand, and remain stable under the pressure. So let's begin with
scalability.
Scalability refers to a system's ability to handle growth without failing. That growth
could be more users, more requests, or even more data being processed. A scalable
system continues to meet performance expectations as the demand increases.
This matters because real-world traffic is never predictable. An application may work
perfectly at normal load. But during a sudden spike, like a sale or a viral event, the
demand can increase dramatically.
If the architecture cannot handle that growth, the system will fail. There are two main
ways systems scale. The first is vertical scalability, which we also call as scale-up.
This approach increases the power of a single machine by adding more CPUs, more
memories, or faster storage. It's simple to implement and often requires little to no
application change. However, vertical scaling has clear limits.
Hardware can only grow so much. Scaling often requires downtime. And high -end
machines are expensive.
So this approach is commonly used for traditional databases and monolithic
applications. The second approach is horizontal scalability, or which we also refer to
as scale -out. Instead of making one machine bigger, we add more machines and
distribute the workload across them.
Horizontal scaling offers major advantages. There are no theoretical upper limit,
availability improvements, or any fault tolerance increases. Scaling happened without
the downtime.
This model is ideal for web applications, microservers, or NoSQL databases also. A
simple way to remember this is vertical scaling makes one worker stronger. Horizontal
scaling hires more workers.
So cloud environments strongly favor horizontal scaling. Now let's move to elasticity.
Scalability tells us whether a system can grow.

Elasticity tells us whether the system can grow and shrink automatically, based on the
demand. So elastic systems scale up when the traffic increases and scales down when
the traffic drops, without any manual intervention. This is crucial because cloud
workloads fluctuate constantly.

Cloud platforms support elasticity through services like autoscaling groups and
autoscalers. These tools monitor metrics such as CPU usage or request volume and
adjust the capacity automatically. Elasticity helps avoid two extremes.
Over-provisioning wastes money by running unused resources. Under -provisioning
hurts performance and user experience. So elastic systems strike the balance by
delivering the performance when needed and saving cost when demand drops.
This is why elasticity is essential for platforms like food delivery applications or any
kind of ride -sharing services, streaming platforms, or even for flash sales. So to
summarize, scalability enables growth. Horizontal scaling is preferred in the cloud.
Elasticity adds automation and cost efficiency. Together, these concepts form the
foundation of cloud-native architecture.
