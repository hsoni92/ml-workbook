Module Introduction Transcript
This module is extremely important because containers are the foundation of how
modern applications are built and deployed today. Up to now, we've talked a lot about
virtual machines, networking, and infrastructure. Those concepts are still relevant, but
containers change how applications run on top of that infrastructure.
Before containers became popular, teams faced some very common problems.
Applications worked on developers' laptops, but the problem was it failed in production.
Deployments were slow, fragile, and very hard to repeat, and scaling the applications
meant managing servers one by one.
That is when containers were created to solve these exact problems. At a high level,
containers allow you to package an application along with everything it needs to run.
Now, it could be the code, libraries, dependencies, everything into a single unit.
That unit can run consistently on any machine anywhere. A helpful way to think about
this is housing. Virtual machines are like renting the entire house.
Each one has its own setup, its own overhead. Containers are more like furnished
apartments inside a big building. In this module, we'll first understand what containers
really are and why they are lighter than the virtual machines.
Then, we'll look at Docker, the most popular tool used to build and run containers.
After that, we'll introduce Kubernetes and understand why orchestration is required
once you have many containers running together. The goal here is not to overwhelm
you with commands or deep internals.
The goal is to build a clear intuition so when you hear words like Docker, ECR, or
Kubernetes later on, they actually make sense to you. So, let's begin by understanding
the most basic question of all. What exactly are containers? Why are they considered
lightweight compared to virtual machines? Let's try to explore these areas.
